In this episode we will talk about the construction of the history of Christmas, trying to understand the connections of this holiday with other rituals from other mythologies, and then its astronomical foundations, which falls shortly after the darkest day of the year (in the northern hemisphere). We will talk about stars that were not stars, and perhaps not even comets; of three kings who were not kings, and who were not three; of calendar choices dictated by the Sun and opposed to the Sun; of prophecies and astrologers, of planets and divinities.

The construction of Christmas

The Church of Rome and the branches of the Protestant Reformation celebrate on December 25 of the Gregorian calendar each year the feast of the Nativity, that is, the birth of Jesus, considered the Son of God by these religions. The Russian Orthodox Church, the Serbian one, and the Church of Jerusalem also celebrate this feast on December 25, but since they adopt the Julian calendar, there is a difference of 13 days between the dates on which the same feast is celebrated between them and the rest of the Christian world, which is equivalent to January 7 of the Gregorian calendar.

The first things we can ask ourselves are:

1) what exactly does the Christian religion celebrate on Christmas day?

2) since when has December 25 been celebrated?

To answer these and other questions we will have to return to the initial sources of early Christianity, when this religion was not yet organised and hierarchical, and in which it seems it was not fundamental to understand the date of Jesus’ birth. The Gospels, that is, the works we have available to obtain information about the life of Jesus, do not speak of the date of his birth. The Gospels that speak of the so-called “Nativity” are two: that of Matthew and that of Luke, two of the four figures defined as “evangelists.” The Gospel of Luke is dated around 80 CE, while that of Matthew dates around 70 CE. In those years the common sentiment of the religious communities of Christian inspiration was directed more toward the death and resurrection of Christ than toward his birth. Initially therefore, that is, for the first century and a half after the death of Christ (datable around the year 30 CE) no one celebrated his birth on a specific day. One of the first to pose the problem of Jesus’ birth is Origen , a theologian originally from Alexandria in Egypt born in 185 CE. Despite his opinion that “only pagans celebrated the day of their own birth,” he nonetheless has something to say about the story of the Nativity. We will encounter him again when we try to understand which celestial body the Magi observed during their journey to Bethlehem.

The concept of Epiphany, that is, of the manifestation of divinity in the world, is a concept elaborated by the Christian Gnostic tradition, interested in obtaining salvation through knowledge, rather than through the tradition of orthodoxy. The Gnostic sect of Egyptian Christians called the Basilideans, founded in Alexandria, Egypt in the II century CE, celebrated this manifestation on the day of Jesus’ baptism, and did so on January 6, spending the vigil of this feast reading sacred texts. Sextus Julius Africanus , a Roman writer datable between the 2nd and 3rd centuries CE, wrote in the Chronographíai (chronography of the world), that the incarnation of Jesus had occurred on March 25, 1 CE, and that his birth had occurred exactly nine months later, that is, December 25. This dating is influenced by two factors.

The first, of astronomical origin, is due to the fact that March 25 was considered at the time the day of the spring equinox, that is, the day from which the hours of daylight in a day exceeded those of darkness, a phenomenon that was well connected to the coming of Jesus, who would free the world from the darkness of paganism. The second, properly literary, betrays the fact that in antiquity it was customary to trace the birth and death, and other relevant events in the life of an important individual, to the same day. Here we begin to glimpse the relationship that exists with astral mythologies and ritual calendars. A text from 243 CE, titled De Pascha computus , by an author who goes by the name of Pseudo-Cyprian , gives the date of Jesus’ birth as March 28. This work is presented as the improvement of a previous work by Hippolytus of Rome, a Roman theologian, who had arrived at the date of April 2 CE. Works of this kind accumulate, until the IV century CE, when in the East the date accepted by all becomes January 6. In the West the debate is also influenced by the presence of contemporary solar cults, similarly to what happens in the East. In Rome, however, such cults are also embraced by the senatorial elite and the imperial court, which already with Elagabalus , emperor from 218 to 222 CE and by hereditary succession high priest of El-Gabal (the Sun god), had seen the cult of the sun introduced with the feast of Sol Invictus. In the following decades the cult will be associated with other divinities we will discuss, such as Helios and Mithras. In 274 CE the emperor Aurelian officially establishes the cult of Natalis Solis Invicti , that is, of the birth of the Unconquered Sun, which falls on December 25. In that period in fact the winter solstice fell precisely on that day, which therefore was perfect for celebrating a solar feast, and the return of the Sun god, never defeated.

In 336 the local Church of Rome establishes December 25 as the Dies Natalis Christi . The overlap of this feast with the Natalis Solis Invicti is already noted by the Chronograph of 354 , a text on the Roman calendar. In 379 the date is also attested by the Church of Constantinople, in 386 by that of Antioch, and subsequently in the 5th century CE by the Church of Egypt, and in the 6th by that of Jerusalem. Today the Armenian Church is one of the few to celebrate the Nativity of Jesus on January 6. The contrast between the two feasts is also noted by the Doctors of the Church: Augustine of Hippo admonishes Christians who celebrate the Sun in the last days of the year, and exhorts them to worship not “the Sun, but [...] he who created the Sun.” Leo I , pope from 440 to 461 CE, exhorts the faithful not to worship the Sun. Despite this, beneath St. Peter’s Basilica, Jesus is depicted on a chariot, in a work titled Christo Sole (Christ the Sun).

Let’s take a step back: why is it that, despite the multiplicity of dates proposed during the first centuries of Christian history, the most important Churches of East and West chose the two days of January 6 and December 25 as the dates of Jesus’ birth, and then (almost) all converged on the second? Let’s start with Epiphanius of Salamis , a 4th century CE bishop in Salamis, on the island of Cyprus, who in Adversus haereses (Against Heresies), speaks of the night between January 5 and 6, and of January 6 two years later as the arrival of the Magi. He goes so far as to suggest a specific date: the night of January 5, 2 BCE.

Epiphanius connects the birth of Jesus to the date of the winter solstice, shifting it 13 days from it; according to him this shift is explained by the fact that 13 is also the number of the apostles plus Jesus himself. Moreover, since pagans also celebrate on that date the birth of Aion ( Aeon ), a divinity linked to cyclical time and subsequently connected by Neoplatonic scholars with Dionysus , he recognizes in this the fact that even pagans “recognize a part of the truth.” Regarding the cult of Aion on the night between January 5 and 6, Epiphanius says: “In Alexandria, in the so-called Koreion: this is a large temple, seat of the sanctuary of Kore. All night long [the worshippers of the cult] stay awake and sing toward the idol with songs and flutes. At the end of the nocturnal celebration, at the crowing of the rooster, they descend into the underground sanctuary bearing torches and carry a wooden idol which they place naked on a litter.” and again: “Today, at this hour, Kore, the Virgin, gives birth to Aion. [...] And this also happens in the city of Petra... in the temple of the idol, they worship the Virgin in the Arabic language, calling her Khaabu, which translates to Kore or “virgin”; and the one who is born [they call] Dusares, which means “the only one begotten by the Lord”.”

Bright note in the margin #11

The concept of the vigil of a feast, and its celebration starting from the night before it, is connected to the Jewish tradition, in which the following day began from the sunset of the previous one. It is for this reason therefore that over time the tradition of celebrating the vigil of feasts has been codified.

Jerome , bishop of Stridon in the IV century CE, is convinced that the birth of Jesus could only have occurred on the day of the solstice, because of cosmic harmony. The tradition elaborates further, and John Chrysostom , patriarch of Constantinople in the IV century CE, writes a treatise titled “ De solstitiis et aequinoctiis ” ( On the Solstices and Equinoxes ) in which he associates with each “cardinal point of terrestrial astronomy” a biblical meaning: Jesus is conceived during the spring equinox and born on the day of the winter solstice, while John the Baptist is conceived during the autumn equinox, and born during the summer solstice (it is no coincidence that the feast celebrating John the Baptist falls on June 24). Let’s return to the feast of dies natalis solis invicti , which was celebrated on December 25: if we want to truly connect it to the winter solstice, then its origin must be even more ancient than the II century CE, because in the calendar in use at the time the winter solstice had been on December 25 during the 3rd and 2nd centuries BCE! It is therefore probable that this celebration is in turn a revival of a more ancient cult: in Egypt the birth of Horus had long been celebrated around the winter solstice, preparing lentils , which still today are present during this period among typical foods. All of this, together with the fact that all pagan celebrations linked to the solstice were celebrated at the end of December and not at the beginning of January, led the Church of Rome, and as we have seen also almost all the Eastern Churches, to converge on the date of December 25.

Bright note in the margin #12

It is known that prehistoric cultures of central and northern Europe gave great importance to the solstices. This is testified by:

- the Goseck circle in Germany (4800 BCE),

- the mound and passage tomb of Newgrange in Ireland (3150 BCE),

- Stonehenge (3rd millennium BCE),

- the Sky Disc of Nebra (1600 BCE), to mention only the most famous examples.

Other brief examples:

- Point Barrow, in the north of Alaska: the Eksimos worshipped the moment when the sun reappears to chase away the evil spirit Tuna, during winter solstice.

- the Yule log is said to lighten the labor that the sun makes to rekindle its sparkling light.

The Magi

Dieter Koch , in The Star of Bethlehem , speaks of the story of the Magi without committing himself to its veracity. Nonetheless, the story of the Nativity concerns them, and an astronomical history of the Nativity cannot avoid discussing them. Let’s start with the meaning of Magi: the word we find in Matthew is μάγος ( magos ), which means magician, that is, one who performs magic. At the time a magos was one who belonged to the class of priests of the Zoroastrian religion, which had its origins in Persia. It does not therefore seem correct to translate them as “kings” or “wise men,” because the respective translations of βασιλεῖς ( basileus , kings) or σοφοί (wise men) are not found in Matthew. Why then has tradition reported the Magi as “wise men” or “kings”? Probably, says Koch, because of the evil nature that magic had for men of the Church, associated as it was by those same men with the work of an evil entity. Moreover, in various passages of the Old Testament there are prophecies concerning the way in which powerful men, that is, kings, will have to prostrate themselves to the “Son of God.” Despite this, there would be doubts about the origin of the Magi from Persia, deriving from an analysis of the gifts that Matthew says they brought to Jesus: myrrh and incense do not come from Persia, but from the lands of the Nabataeans, in northwestern Arabia. If Matthew was referring, speaking of the Magi, to men coming from the east, as Isaiah also says with a parallel with the rising of the sun in the east, then the Nabataeans would also be a possible source for the Magi, since in the Old Testament the east is a way to indicate precisely Arabia.

Origen believes that the Magi come from Mesopotamia, Justin and Tertullian instead that they come from Damascus; Persian legends instead claim that they originated from Ecbatana , today Hamadan, which had been the capital of the Medes. In any case, their nationality cannot be stated with certainty. One can say however, returning to the etymology, that magos in ancient Persian is magush , which is the original name of an ancient tribe of the Medes. The religion of this people was polytheistic, linked to the Indian Vedic religion, until about 1000 BCE, when Zoroaster introduced monotheism. And Zoroaster himself has a prophecy that serves our case, and that could explain why Magi who followed that doctrine would have decided to undertake a journey toward Palestine.

Zoroaster

Zoroaster himself had predicted that at a certain point a Saoshyant , that is a savior, would arrive on earth, defeating the forces of evil and bringing about a state of peace and blessing. He would then kill a bull, extract fat from it and produce a decoction (?) that would make humans immortal. Moreover, this man was to be born of a virgin. Once again etymology tells an interesting story: the word in Avestan saoshyant , which translates as “he who will save,” becomes sô-sont- in Greek, and also has connections with sô-têr , which translates as “saviour.”

Mithras

Perhaps Matthew was alluding to Mithras ?

Mithras is an ancient god venerated by the Persians and ancient Indians. In the Zoroastrian religion, Mithras becomes the judge of souls, and the story told that Ahura Mazda, the wise god, the most powerful figure in the Zoroastrian religion, had created Mithras in his image, in analogy with what happens in the Christian religion. The cult of Mithras was long in competition, in the East but also in Rome, with the Christian religion of the time. And the analogies with the figure of Christ continue: Mithras is born in a cave, Jesus in a stable (but the stables of Bethlehem were carved into the rock).

James George Frazer in The Golden Bough speaks of fertility cults in Western Asia (Near East) emphasising the points of contact between the cult of Mithras, that of the “mother of the gods,” and the Christian religion. Speaking of the cult of the sun in Syria and Egypt, he speaks of the faithful who at midnight came out of the sanctuaries shouting “The Virgin has given birth! The Light grows!” He speaks of the representations of the newly (re)born Sun as a child, and of the mother of the child Sun god as a Celestial Virgin, a divinity similar to the figures of Astarte (Ashtoreth, as in Deuteronomy).

The phenomenon in the sky

We have spoken of the date of Jesus’ birth, we have spoken of some analogies with Semitic cults and with Persian ones. Where does astronomy enter into the story of the Nativity? We will try to represent the various possibilities that astronomy proposes to us, starting from the texts we have available on the description of the Nativity that have within them some reference to some celestial object. First of all, most studies on the “star” of the Nativity speak of an extraordinary celestial phenomenon or a particular astrological configuration: this is motivated by the fact that it is improbable that a “simple” event or a usual astrological configuration would have motivated the Magi to undertake the journey described by Matthew. But the possibilities are multiple, according to Koch:

1) it could be an unusual and very rare event;

2) it could be a special astrological configuration, difficult to interpret for common men, but interpretable by the Magi;

3) it could have been a very ordinary phenomenon, which however had the function of indicating, in the right context - that of a Zoroastrian or Hebrew prophecy - the approach of a particularly special event.

Obviously, the possibility that there was nothing of all this and that the story of the “star” and the Magi is just a story hovers over us and over History. Despite this, the fact that it was told in this way, and not in another, could justify the desire to understand it thoroughly and try to interpret it. Matthew writes: “After Jesus was born in Bethlehem of Judea, in the time of King Herod, behold, some Magi came from the east to Jerusalem and said: ‘Where is he who has been born, the king of the Jews? We have seen his star rising [in the east] and have come to worship him’” (Matthew 2:1) “Then Herod, having secretly called the Magi, ascertained from them exactly the time when the star had appeared” (Matthew 2:7) “And behold, the star, which they had seen rising, went before them, until it came and stood over the place where the child was.” (Matthew 2:9) These are the only phrases that speak of an object in the sky that “indicated” to the Magi that Jesus would be born in Bethlehem. Let’s try to understand what they tell us. Koch here speaks of the expression translated as “rising [in the east],” saying that the word for east, anatolê , can also be translated as “ rising ,” and not only as “east.” Anatolê in fact is used in relation to the rising of the Sun, the Moon, or a star. The word Anatolia indicates, from Greek, “the land of dawn.” So the Magi say they saw a star rising in the east, not necessarily that they were in the east when they saw a star. Now, it is useful to understand what it means that a star rose in the east. Aren’t the same stars always in the sky, night after night? During the year, most stars, except the so-called “circumpolar stars,” or imperishable stars for the ancient Egyptians, disappear in the evening sky to the west and appear only after a few weeks in the morning sky. The moment when a star reappears is called heliacal rising , because the star is visible for a short time together with the rising Sun, which being brighter than any other object in the sky visible from Earth, covers the light of every other celestial body. After that first rising, the star will be visible every day a little earlier, until it is visible even before dawn. The heliacal rising of a star occurs on a specific date, and obviously depends on the star. When then Herod asks “exactly the time when the star had appeared,” it is because he wanted to know the exact day when the child was born, connecting the event in the sky to the birth. In the case that the “star” was truly a heliacal rising, then the Magi, who were versed in astronomy, especially if coming from Babylon, would have had all the time to calculate the exact date and then prepare the journey to reach Palestine. On the contrary, in the case that the “star” had been a sudden event, the impossibility of calculating it in advance makes it impossible to prepare the journey in advance. But a journey of this kind (from Babylon to Palestine) would have taken a couple of months, and the text does not say this.

When the Magi leave Jerusalem to go toward Bethlehem, the text says that the star “went before them.” This should mean, given that Jerusalem and Bethlehem are on a north-south axis, that the star would have had to be found looking toward the south. But the star is to the south only in full daylight, because after the heliacal rising we said that the star always rises in the east, always a little before the Sun, until it is visible even at night. The term for “went before” in Greek is προῆγεν ( proêgen ), which means “to bring forward.” Where else is this word used? Matthew uses it in its meaning of “to go ahead,” or “to bring before a judge.” However, in New Testament language the verb is also used in place of προηγεῖσθαι ( pro(h)egeisthai ), which it does mean “to precede,” but also, from an astronomical point of view, “to be retrograde,” referring to a retrograde motion of a planet. Ptolemy in the Tetrabiblos and Geminus in Introduction to the Phenomena use it in this way. What is a retrograde motion ?

The planets move, observing them at night, in the same direction in which the Sun moves during the day. Moreover, all the planets known to the ancients moved in a band around the Sun, called the zodiacal band. Sometimes it seems however that some planets do not move in the “normal” direction, but seem to go backward - that is, they seem to “walk backward.” If therefore we accept the astronomical interpretation of pro(h)egeisthai , the “star” cannot have been a star as we understand it, but only a planet, since stars do not show retrograde motion. Moreover, it had to be a planet that presents retrograde motion during its heliacal rising. This planet is Venus .

When the text says that the star stopped over the place where the child was, we can interpret the text with a phenomenon known to ancient astrologers as “station of a planet.” This phenomenon occurs when a planet seems to “stop” relative to the motion of the other planets of the zodiacal band, to reverse its own motion, from retrograde to direct and vice versa. This interpretation is in accordance with the planetary nature of the “star” we are looking for, and also with the fact that, the station of the planet occurring a few days after its heliacal rising, everything must have happened when the Magi were already in Palestine. Koch speaks of the fact that Matthew’s failure to use the astronomical term for planetary station, στηριγμός ( stērigmos ), is due to the fact that Matthew was not an astronomer.Accepting that the star was Venus, why doesn’t Matthew say its name? The most immediate reason seems to be that the name of the planet, Ashteret, was associated with a pagan divinity (Astarte), and that for this reason, as is often done in the Old Testament, its name is not written. Moreover, in Hebrew and Aramaic the planet Venus was also simply called “the star,” or the “she-star,” that is kōbāb/kōkäbät and kaukab/kaukabta , respectively.

Other versions

After interpreting the verses of Matthew reported above, C.J. Humphreys writes, in The Star of Bethlehem , that the characteristics of the star can be listed as follows:

1) it is a new star, one that was not visible before;

2) it moved slowly in the sky, appearing in different points of the sky at different times;

3) at a certain point it seemed to stop above Bethlehem, in the sense that seen from Jerusalem, the star seemed to be located above the place where Bethlehem is.

The ideas that Humphreys initially proposes concern the conjunction of two planets, a nova, and a comet.

A conjunction

Starting from the 8th century CE, the Jewish astronomer Māshāʾallāh speaks of a planetary conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn that occurred in the 7th century BCE. The problem, according to Humphreys, is that this explanation is not attested by anyone else before him; Koch adds that the problems also concern the very nature of the conjunction: at the moment of conjunction, the planets would have had to be distant on the celestial vault by a couple of lunar diameters, that is, a distance at which the planets are still distinguishable, and which therefore would hardly have made that conjunction a star. Moreover, accepting the interpretation of heliacal rising, retrograde motion, and stationarity, a planetary conjunction satisfies none of these.

A nova

Jean-François Foucquet (1665-1741), French Jesuit and scientist, as a century earlier Johannes Kepler (1571-1630), German astronomer, writes that a nova could have been the star, in particular a nova that exploded in conjunction with the conjunction of Jupiter and Saturn in 7 BCE. A nova is a phenomenon that occurs when the brightness of a star suddenly increases, causing that same star, which before was perhaps barely visible, to appear much brighter than before to an observer.

The problem with this theory is that events of this type are impossible to predict, and that therefore there was no way for the Magi to prepare a journey and then arrive in Jerusalem and observe the nova. Moreover, a nova is a phenomenon concerning stars, which do not have motions observable in an evident way, such as to make the Magi say that the star “guided” them. Furthermore, in Kepler’s time the difference between a nova and a comet was still imperceptible, since it was thought that novae were comets without tails. And it was precisely Kepler who hypothesised that novae, not moving relative to the sky of fixed stars, belonged to this same sphere, unlike comets, which instead inhabit the spheres closer to Earth. But not only in Europe was this difference still imperceptible: there exist sources in China that speak of comets around 5 or 4 BCE, interpretable both in the current meaning of the term and as novae stars. And it is precisely a comet, the celestial body candidate, together with Venus, to play the role of “star” of the Nativity.

A comet

Already starting from the 2nd century CE, Origen speaks of a comet as a possible candidate for the role of “star” present in the gospel of Matthew. The theory on the nature of comets dated back to Aristotle, according to whom they were objects that belonged to the sub-lunar sphere, that lived in the upper part of the atmosphere. They were signs of omen, not necessarily of bad omen, but for example Dio Cassius , when he describes the passage of a comet (which was probably Halley’s comet) does so by relating it to the death of Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa , Roman general (63 BCE -12 BCE). The first comet whose passage can be predicted is Halley’s comet, named in relation to the English astronomer who hypothesised its predictability after observing it in 1705, confirmed in 1759. A prediction of this kind was not possible at the time of the 1st century BCE - and until the invention of the telescope, at least - therefore the Magi could not have used the prediction of a comet to prepare their journey. Koch uses this argument, among others, to speak of the impossibility that the “star” followed by the Magi was a comet. Humphreys is not of the same opinion, and investigates the phenomenon; he does so by searching the Chinese archives for a trace of phenomena attributable to comets dated around the years in which, from the accounts we have, the possible birth of Jesus is dated. He reduces the search to three phenomena:

(1) a comet, po-hsing , which passed around August 12 BCE (which is Halley’s comet), visible for just under 60 days;

(2) a sui-hsing comet, visible between March and April of 5 BCE;

(3) another po-hsing comet, visible around April 4 BCE.

Using the information contained in the gospel of Luke, Humphreys proceeds thus: since Jesus is said to have been about 30 years old at the time of baptism by John the Baptist (Luke 3:23), who had begun his ministry during the fifteenth year of Tiberius’ reign (Luke 3:1-2), a possible date for the aforementioned baptism could be the year 29 CE or the period between autumn 28 and autumn 29 CE. This means, interpreting the expression “about 30 years” as between 26 and 34 years, that the earliest date for Jesus’ birth is 7 BCE. This eliminates the possibility that Halley’s comet in its passage of 12 BCE represents the “star” of the nativity.

What instead is the latest date for the birth? It is obtained from the date of death of King Herod the Great, who is said to have died in March 4 BCE. Since the comet of April 4 BCE is subsequent to this date, and we know that Jesus, from the accounts of the gospels, was born during Herod’s reign, then we can also eliminate the comet dating to this date. Therefore remains the comet that appeared around March 5 BCE. Humphreys, from the accounts of the comet’s appearances, reconstructs the following: it seems to have appeared in the countries that today make up Arabia around early March, in an area of the sky that includes the zone where the constellation of Capricorn is drawn; it was visible from these countries in the first lights of dawn; its motion is compatible with the Jerusalem-Bethlehem axis, since it seems to have had its tail along that direction, moving toward the north. Accepting that this is the comet observed by the Magi, who from Babylon to Jerusalem had time to travel during the comet’s path in the sky, which lasted just under two months, was such an omen sufficient to mobilize them? Humphreys connects the comet to other celestial phenomena, in particular to two others: the first is the conjunction of Saturn and Jupiter in 7 BCE, the second the proximity of three planets in 6 BCE. These phenomena seemed to prepare the arrival of an additional and definitive symbol: the comet in Capricorn in 5 BCE. Why?

Isaac Abarbanel , Jewish philosopher of the XV century CE, writes that in the astrology and mythology of the Magi, Saturn represents the divine father, and Jupiter is his son. Moreover, the third planet of 6 BCE is Mars, which heralds the coming of a warrior king, according to Chaldean astrology (in Babylon); and the comet of 5 BCE crosses Capricorn, which is considered the seat of the Sun’s rebirth, and the daytime dwelling of Saturn. Furthermore, the Magi claim to have seen the star “from the east”: we already know that this interpretation is debated, but accepting it as part of the scholarly community does, Humphreys extracts from it the information that this was the reason for the Magi’s rapid departure. According to Ptolemy in fact, it was common knowledge among astrologers that a comet arriving “from the east” was a prediction of events of imminent realisation , while a comet arriving from the west spoke of events further ahead in time. Finally, even Daniel, Hebrew prophet in exile in Babylon, speaks of a king comparing him to an animal with horns (when he speaks of Alexander the Great). Accepting this interpretation, do the travel times of the Magi’s journey and the vision of the comet in the sky agree? According to Humphreys yes, since traveling between 50 and 100 miles per day, an acceptable average for the means and period, the route of the time from Babylon to Jerusalem first and then Bethlehem, is between 500 and 900 miles. Moreover, Matthew does not say that the Magi followed the comet for the entire journey, but only that it directed them westward, and that it went before them toward Bethlehem. Koch is skeptical about this interpretation for various reasons, among which the fact that comets are surprising phenomena when they are particularly visible, which does not seem to have happened for a comet in that time period, or that Halley’s comet returned to visit Earth in the year 66 CE, when Matthew was perfectly capable of observing it: if he did so, why didn’t he connect this comet to that of 5 BCE, perhaps announcing a second coming of Christ, as all Christians in those first years after his death expected?

Venus as a star

Accepting Koch’s interpretation on the nature of the “star” of the nativity as Venus, let’s try to understand why Matthew, despite never naming the planet, could have chosen such a celestial body as a symbol of Jesus’ birth. First of all, Koch connects the planet Venus to the divinities of Western Asia, particularly in Mesopotamia, with the goddess Ishtar, and in the kingdom of the Assyrians. In these regions the goddess had slightly different names, such as Ishtar, Astarte, Athart, and was associated with the role of protector and creator of kings.

Bright note in the margin #13

Incidentally, the representation of the virgin woman with the child god, in the figure of the Virgin Mary with the Baby Jesus, falls within the tradition of the relationship between the king and the divinity that protects him, or in the representation of fertility. Examples are the divinities in Assyria and Babylon that protected the king from birth, and the relationship between the young Horus and the goddess Isis.

Then, after framing the planet as the brightest celestial body in the sky after the Sun and Moon, and which precisely with the other two bodies in Babylon forms a triad of astral divinities (respectively Ishtar, Shamash and Sin), Koch speaks of Venus as the symbol of resurrection from death, due to the fact that it is at the same time Hesperus and Phosphorus, respectively the evening star, which goes to die toward the west, and the morning star, which is reborn in the east.

Bright note in the margin #14

Jerome, one of the fathers of the church, states that, in the place that tradition recognizes as the site of Jesus’ birth, the local community of faithful, from the times of Hadrian until the reign of Constantine - that is, just under two centuries - had erected a small sanctuary in honor of Tammuz, that is, Adonis. Adonis, which in Semitic means “lord, master,” was an ancient divinity venerated in Mesopotamia, and was the lover of Ishtar, that is, Venus. George Frazer too, Scottish ethnologist of the 20th century CE, speaks of the “star” of Bethlehem as Venus, morning star, connecting it to the cult of Adonis observed in the area.

Venus is a “star” of particular importance not only in traditions that Christians consider pagan: even in the Bible, despite not being called by name, there are references to this figure. In Revelation 22:16, the author reports the words of Jesus who states that he is ὁ ἀστὴρ ὁ λαμπρὸς ὁ πρωϊνός, that is, the bright morning star . Also in a verse from the second letter of Peter (II Peter 1:19), the author speaks of Jesus comparing him to the morning star, that is, Venus .

Which date, finally?

We have already spoken of the fact that it is unlikely that Jesus’ birth occurred on December 25, and we are far from being able to arrive at a specific date. The decision to select a specific date is usually attributed to the need to define an orthodoxy, in a period subsequent to the first centuries after the beginning of the Christian cult, aimed at differentiating the day of Jesus’ birth from that of his manifestation in the world, which first the Gnostics and then the Manichaeans had raised as a critical point of Christian historiography. The date of December 25 seems to have been agreed upon subsequently by the Church of Rome first, and then by almost all the others, for various reasons, mainly linked to the presence of previous or contemporary solar cults, such as the cult of Sol Invictus, or that of Mithras, and can be inserted within the tradition of celebrating the winter solstice.
