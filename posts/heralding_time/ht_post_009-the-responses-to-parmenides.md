There are those who will defend his theses, and those who will attack them: but both camps are included in the category of <em>post-Parmenidean</em> philosophers.</p><pre><code><code>This Christmas, become a paid subscriber to this newsletter to support my work!</code></code></pre><pre><code><code>Thanks! And if you don't feel like becoming a paid subscriber, share this project with others that may be interested!</code></code></pre><div><hr></div><h3><strong>The post-Parmenideans</strong></h3><h4>Zeno</h4><p>We will not talk too much about Zeno, because he himself was not interested in developing or commenting on cosmological models. We know he was born around 490 BCE<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1" target="_self">1</a>, and that he was a disciple of Parmenides in Elea, the city where he lived and taught. Plato informs us that he wrote to defend the theses of his master Parmenides, and Aristotle speaks of him as the father of dialectic<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-2" href="#footnote-2" target="_self">2</a>.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xNKX!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5a5e9600-5bb9-45a1-b4b4-131ab17e75ec_1240x1260.png" alt=""></figure><h4>Empedocles</h4><p>The first of the two philosophers we will discuss who were critical of Parmenides, Empedocles has his <em>acme</em> around the LXXXIV Olympiad, so it seems he was born around 482 BCE<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-3" href="#footnote-3" target="_self">3</a>, or in 492 BCE, and died around 432 BCE. Originally from Agrigento, a Greek city in Sicily, he visits Thurii and probably meets the Pythagoreans; he is then exiled to the Peloponnese.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!--YT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F701fde01-e347-4224-988f-70da5afe792e_1240x1178.png" alt=""></figure><p>Diogenes Laertius writes that to prove he was the son of a god, he threw himself into the crater of Mount Etna.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TN0u!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1ad9f455-9197-498d-8494-6d109c1e9209_1230x1664.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>The death of Empedocles, work by Salvator Rosa (1670). Image obtained fromPrivate collection (Helen Langdon, Salvator Rosa - Dulwich Picture Gallery). Credits <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=59985344">here</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>He writes two works, "On Nature" and "Purifications", although some maintain they are the same text. Only fragments remain, and accepting the division into two books, the first contains physical explanations about the Universe, while the second the explanation of the Pythagorean doctrine of the transmigration of souls. We will speak of the first work, which concerns his cosmological ideas.</p><p>Undergoing an evident influence from Parmenides, Empedocles&#8217; interest is first of all to answer the questions posed by him: (i) things exist because they have always existed, therefore there is no need for them to be born from nothing, which cannot exist; (iii) the plurality of the world does not come from Unity, but from the plurality of things, that is from water, air, earth and fire; (ii) these four elements completely fill the Universe, which therefore does not contain the void around it, since this cannot exist; (iv) motion is due to the presence of two &#8220;forces,&#8221; <em>Love</em> and <em>Strife</em>.</p><pre><code>Bright note in the margin #9

The concept of perennial unity even in multiplicity serves Empedocles also to explain the birth of humanity.
For the philosopher the sexes are produced by the division of creatures &#8220;of full nature,&#8221; subsequently divided into males and females.
In the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad the Universe begins as a unity in human form, in a very similar way.
&#8220;Now it was of the size of a man and a woman locked in an embrace. It divided itself in two, and from this man and woman were born.&#8221;</code></pre><p>Empedocles&#8217; cosmology begins with a single sphere that already contains the plurality of elements, since nothing can be born or perish, but only be <em>altered</em>. These elements are called &#8220;roots,&#8221; and are eternal, in fixed quantities, and do not transform or change in any way. However, they are not elements as we understand them today in physics and chemistry: we can consider them as we today understand the states of matter - solid, liquid, gaseous, plasma. To water is associated the divinity <em>Nestis</em> (Persephone), to fire Zeus, to air <em>Hera</em>, and to earth <em>Aidoneus</em>, probably Hades.</p><p>Then comes the ether, fire, earth, and water. Last comes air, and all things are born: the firmament from ether, the sun from fire, terrestrial things from water, air and earth. From these elements, and thanks to Love and Strife, things assume their form: are these two concepts <em>forces</em>, or <em>physical objects</em>? It is not clear, but it seems they represent tendencies, which are added to the <em>natural</em> ones of the elements: for example, fire and air naturally tend <em>to rise</em>, while water and earth tend <em>to descend</em>; to this are added the tendencies of things <em>to unite </em>and<em> dissolve</em> according to the periods the world goes through, whether of greater <em>Love</em> or greater <em>Strife</em>.</p><p>A&#235;tius reports that for Empedocles the stars are made of fire, and are fixed on the celestial vault, as Anaximenes already said. The celestial vault, formed by air solidified or frozen" by fire, is not a perfect sphere, but has the shape of an egg.</p><p>While the stars are fixed, the planets are free to move around the Earth, and one of them is the Sun, which is not made of fire, but reflects part of it.</p><p>The alternation of day and night and of the seasons is explained by the existence of two hemispheres, one of fire and the other of air, in which there is some fire, which rotate around the Earth. Eclipses are due to the passage of the Earth in front of the Moon.</p><p>These two hemispheres do not have equal size, and this is the reason for the different length of the seasons during the year; moreover, it is precisely the revolutionary motion of these two hemispheres that keeps the Earth stationary in its place at the center of the Universe.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VpTr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8266ad8b-93c4-47f3-b6f5-a188c6daa575_800x954.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>Illustration from a 1587 treatise on comets and meteors, created anonymously in Flanders (now northern France) and known as the <em>Kometenbuch</em>. Immagine ottenuta da <a href="https://pdimagearchive.org/images/8ad8e3f0-81b1-4a3e-8905-ce2187f03325">Public Domain Image Archive / ORKA - Open Repository Kassel</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>The Moon is a reflection of the light of the Sun, and is formed by frozen air, surrounded by a sphere of fire; it has the shape of a disk, and this comment does not allow us to understand if the shape of the celestial bodies - and of the Earth itself - is for Empedocles a flat disk or a sphere. In any case, the Sun is larger than the Moon, but despite this it is twice as far from Earth as the Moon is. The light of the celestial bodies reaches us after crossing the distance between the fire and the Earth.</p><p>Empedocles therefore does not completely reject Parmenides&#8217; ideas, but affirms that change is observable, and that it really occurs in the world, generating new things from the mixture of different elements.</p><h4><strong>Anaxagoras</strong></h4><p>Anaxagoras of Clazomenae, a city in Ionia, was born around 500 BCE<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-4" href="#footnote-4" target="_self">4</a>. During his life he moves to Athens, bringing for the first time the philosophy of Asia Minor and Magna Graecia to the polis, and works closely with Pericles. He has the distinction of being the first philosopher of whom there is news to have been condemned for <em>impiety</em>, for having stated that the Sun is a burning stone, and not a divinity. Unlike Socrates, he goes into exile in Lampsacus - a city of the Troad in Asia Minor - and dies there around 428 BCE.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Auuh!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8ef0fb0f-da75-42db-90a7-334a40ccf203_1240x1264.png" alt=""></figure><p>Diogenes Laertius reports that for Anaxagoras the task of humanity is "<em>to question the Sun, the Moon and the firmament".</em></p><p>From the information we have, it seems he wrote a work in prose titled &#8220;Physica,&#8221; that is &#8220;Studies on Nature.&#8221; Certainly later than Parmenides&#8217; period of activity, from him he accepts the division between what is and what is not, and the renunciation of the concept of creation and destruction of things in the world.</p><p>Similarly to Empedocles, Anaxagoras considers change the work of <em>separation</em> and <em>mixture</em>, but unlike him, he considers <em>separation</em> the only cause of <em>destruction</em>, and <em>mixture</em> as the only cause for <em>creation</em>.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TrsJ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffb2d62ce-fb0f-4d6f-869a-5572c3487b31_1070x1200.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>A Representation of the Convexity, if I may call it so, work by Thomas Wright (1750). Image obtained from <a href="https://pdimagearchive.org/images/29f68cd3-d6e3-47ed-9c6a-a07b707f1fdd">Public Domain Image Archive / Internet Archive / The Getty</a></figcaption></figure><p>His system provides for five entities: (1) <em>macroscopic</em> objects, (2) <em>simple</em> things, (3) <em>portions</em>, (4) <em>seeds</em>, (5) the <em>Mind</em>. Moreover, there are six principles that govern the world: (i) destruction and creation do not exist, (ii) there exist many, infinite in number, <em>simple</em> things, (iii) there is a portion of everything in everything, (iv) everything is mainly composed of the portion most present in that thing, (v) an infinitesimal portion does not exist, (vi) the NOUS is not mixed and (I) knows everything, (II) governs everything, (III) defines everything in order, (IV) causing its motion.</p><p>In the beginning all things were mixed; the Mind then causes the rotation of everything, creating first in a limited place, and then everywhere, the separation of identifiable objects from the initial mixture. The rotation leads the elements to separate: first the <em>aer</em> and the <em>aether</em> separate, air and ether, present in larger portions. Air tends toward the center, while ether, lighter, toward the periphery of the vortex.</p><p>The heavier things go toward the center, except for the celestial bodies, which although they are burning rocks, remain in the air thanks to the speed of rotation of the vortex, which increases more and more.</p><pre><code><code>Bright note in the margin #10

The description of celestial bodies as burning rocks could have been suggested to Anaxagoras, or found confirmation in it, in the fall of a meteorite at Aegospotami in 467 BCE.</code></code></pre><p>For Anaxagoras there exists an infinite number of portions, therefore they are not comparable to our chemical elements, and these portions, also called roots, are compressed by the rotation due to the vortex and form the solid bodies we find on Earth.</p><p>The Earth is flat, and remains stationary because it is very large, because air supports it, and because the void does not exist. The Sun, the Moon and the planets are burning rocks that orbit in the sky around the Earth, and when we do not see them they orbit <em>beneath</em> it. The Moon is formed of the same materials of which the Earth is made, and its light is a reflection of sunlight; lunar eclipses are caused both by the position of the Earth in relation to it and by smaller bodies that obscure it in their passages.</p><p>It is not clear if for Anaxagoras the contemporary worlds he speaks of are worlds different from Earth, or places of our Earth but inaccessible to us: in any case, he speaks of the Milky Way as a collection of stars, more visible than the other stars in the sky because wrapped in the shadow of the Earth when the Sun passes beneath it. It is not surprising that the Earth has movements: already Anaxagoras, who had also defined the Earth as stationary, supposes that earthquakes occur when the air on which it rests moves because of the rain that infiltrates into the Earth, causing a slight movement of it.</p><h4>Melissus</h4><p>Originally from Samos, probably born around 500 BCE, in his treatise "On Nature" he speaks, like Parmenides, of Being as uncreated, indestructible, indivisible, unchangeable, without motion, and always equal to itself. Unlike Parmenides, however, he defines the world as infinite, not only in time, but also in space.</p><h4><strong>The Atomists</strong></h4><h5><strong>Leucippus and Democritus</strong></h5><p>For Leucippus, a philosopher of whom we know almost nothing except that around 450 BCE he creates a school together with his disciple Democritus, the void exists, and matter is formed by an infinite number of small bodies, which are not divisible (atoms). Among these, the moister atoms with fire become stars. Change therefore is due to the accumulation in one place of particles that already exist from the beginning. The atoms moreover are infinite not only in number but also in shape and size.</p><p>Leucippus&#8217; theories are often proposed together with those of Democritus, since it is difficult to discern the individual contributions. Democritus was born, according to Apollodorus, around 458 BCE, and is as we said a disciple of Leucippus, both active in Abdera. The text of atomism he writes (or they write) is "The Little World System", and everything there is traced back to the shape and dimensions of atoms.</p><p>The cosmology that can be reconstructed for both is the following: two things exist, atoms and the void, and the former move within the latter. Moreover, there exists a distinction between the Universe, which is the totality of what exists, and the Cosmos, which is instead the world system limited in time and space. Infinite worlds exist, and each is born when many bodies of different shapes move toward the void, creating a vortex that makes things separate "according to necessity": the heavier bodies move toward the center, and the Earth is at the center. </p><p>The Sun and Moon rotate around the Earth. The order of celestial bodies for Leucippus is Sun, planets, stars, Moon, and Earth at the center, while for Democritus the order is slightly different: stars, planets, Sun, Moon, Venus and Earth. Eclipses occur when the Earth modifies its own inclination with respect to the zenith.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7w2b!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc75614fd-2da8-46f3-9087-e7e81b809ea4_601x800.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>Colour wheel, featured in his treatise on colour <em>Versuch eines Farbensystems</em>, opera di Ignaz Schifferm&#252;ller (1772). Image obtained from <a href="https://pdimagearchive.org/images/4b100721-37a4-49cb-976c-442f1bf2398a">Public Domain Image Archive / Internet Archive / The Getty</a></figcaption></figure><p>In conclusion, Leucippus creates a physical theory in response to Parmenides, introducing the idea of atoms, indivisible, and Democritus modifies Leucippus&#8217; theory to describe different phenomena.</p><p>The cosmos of the atomists has nothing special, divine, or inevitable about it.</p><h4><strong>Diogenes of Apollonia</strong></h4><p>Born in the Milesian colony of Apollonia in the 5th century BCE, he is considered by Theophrastus "the last natural philosopher", therefore in full tradition of the philosophers of Miletus. Diogenes Laertius counts him among the disciples of Anaximenes, and places his <em>acme</em> between 440 and 430 BCE.</p><p>He considers air divine and identifies it with the origin of the world, and in his cosmology the Earth is flat and stationary at the center of the world, supported by a vortex created by heat generated by the exhalations of the world. He resolves the inconsistency between the affirmation that interaction between bodies does not exist, and the evidence that this interaction occurs, by stating that all things derive from a single substance, which therefore can interact with a part of itself.</p><p>In his cosmology it is the first time we encounter the "argument from design": if all things are designed in the best possible way, at the origin of such an design there must be an intelligent designer".</p><p>The celestial bodies are pumice stones, light and fiery some, light and invisible the others, which sometimes fall to Earth (also for Diogenes this description could concern the meteorite that fell at Aegospotami which we mentioned earlier).</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!j1bX!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4a693a3c-a157-4f4d-9d80-da6f099ac5b7_1240x1146.png" alt=""></figure><div><hr></div><p>We have spoken so far of schools in Miletus, in Elea; we have arrived as far as Athens with the first philosophers to bring cosmology to the polis; we have dealt with philosophers about whom nothing is known, and those for whom the information we have is discordant.</p><p>Arthur Koestler speaks of philosophy in Greece up to this moment as a musical ensemble in which everyone tries to tune their instrument.
