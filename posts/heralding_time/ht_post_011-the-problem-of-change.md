Diogenes Laertius reports 570 BCE as his date of birth, and places the philosopher&#8217;s death at 95 years old, in 475 BCE. Originally from Colophon, a city in Ionia, he lives in Zancle (which today is Messina) and in Catania, and then precisely in Elea.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mOAK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F02e085da-eaf8-419a-b44c-178e7a85a3f4_1240x1228.png" alt=""></figure><p>Although he is not a philosopher from Miletus, his place of origin (Colophon is a few dozen kilometers from Miletus) and his philosophy place him in direct contact with the philosophies of the three philosophers we talked about in the two previous episodes.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dMiW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F104ff0ab-b36a-490e-ba7e-39ea7133e18b_900x1300.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>Xenophanes, figure by Thomas Stanley (1655). The history of philosophy: containing the lives, opinions, actions and Discourses of the Philosophers of every Sect, illustrated with effigies of divers of them. Credits <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senofane#/media/File:Xenophanes_in_Thomas_Stanley_History_of_Philosophy.jpg">here</a>.</figcaption></figure><div><hr></div><p>From the few fragments (40 fragments for a total of about 100 verses) of a poetic work of his, and from other secondhand and thirdhand citations, we know that his idea of the world is in contrast with the vision of Homer and Hesiod based on the anthropomorphism of the gods: for Xenophanes &#8220;God is one,&#8221; there is no longer room for divine intervention in the world in human affairs or in the creation of natural elements and phenomena. For Xenophanes &#8220;God&#8221; is immortal, eternal, does not have human appearance, but is not <em>immaterial</em> for this reason. He therefore poses the question: &#8220;Is He the world, or only a substance in it?&#8221; To answer, he looks at the world and sees that it is full of movements: since God cannot move, then He must not be part of the world, but only permeate it - but he does not explain how.</p><p>Xenophanes does not create a cosmogony, but according to Aristotele<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1" target="_self">1</a>, he believes in the eternity of the world. </p><p>He too, like the philosophers of Miletus, searches for an original substance, and according to him there are two primordial substances: earth, dry, and water, moist.</p><p>A trait that emerges from his writings and from the testimonies of the doxographers is his frequent use of examples: for example, to justify his idea that the world is born from water and earth, and will dissolve into these two "elements", he cites as an example marine fossils found on mountains. In addition, from mud the earth will reform, and the cycle will begin again.</p><p>Regarding his strictly astronomical ideas, he emancipates himself from the Milesian tradition and sees no evidence of the necessity for the Earth to have to rest on something: his Earth is infinite in extension and depth. This generates a consequence for which commentators attribute to him the solution as follows: since such an Earth would be too large for the Sun and Moon to illuminate it all, there exist innumerable celestial bodies called Sun and Moon in different parts of the world, at different latitudes<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-2" href="#footnote-2" target="_self">2</a>.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!JsGQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5101a4d4-7384-43f1-ac7a-4d413e0479fa_864x1200.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>Atlas sustains the Firmament, work by Bernard Picart (1731). Image obtained from <a href="https://pdimagearchive.org/images/5d9d3ffa-ecff-44d6-b689-61889004d33f">Public Domain Image Archive / Rijksmuseum</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>Again, he sees no evidence of why there should be a <em>solid</em> celestial vault: in his Universe the air extends indefinitely upward.</p><p>Hippolytus reports that for Xenophanes the Sun is "new every day", and the Moon every month, and both are formed from small tongues of fire united together, like bright clouds; they form from small exhalations due to the humidity of the air, and therefore come from the Earth. The Sun moreover moves in a straight line, but from Earth it seems to move in a circle around it.</p><div><hr></div><p>What emerges from Xenophanes, and from the other philosophers of Miletus, is the trust, indeed the certainty, that a <em>rational order exists</em> in the world, and that humanity is able to gain knowledge from it. From Hesiod, whom Xenophanes does not love but whom the others do not despise, they nevertheless obtain confirmation that it is necessary to try to understand the world. The fundamental idea is that applying reason to cosmology <em>can be enough,</em> and that nothing else is needed - tradition, religion, dogma - to explain the world. McKirahan writes that for Xenophanes "Hesiod is not suitable"<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-3" href="#footnote-3" target="_self">3</a>. </p><p>The four philosophers seek and find objections to each other&#8217;s theories, and produce new theories that are immune from those criticisms (even if others could be raised). They certainly make some "bold generalisations" that today would not be acceptable, but without the possibility of verifying hypotheses with experiments, or with more accurate observations, it was probably difficult to do better.</p><p>As we also said in the previous episode, they do not use deductive proof" to advance their reasoning: it is necessary, to accept a theory, that its reasoning be plausible.</p><p>On the other hand, when one trusts one&#8217;s means of observation, all that is needed is for the reasoning to be coherent and plausible.</p><p>But what if our eyes were not reliable?</p><div><hr></div><h3><strong>Heraclitus</strong></h3><p>The world of the philosophers of Miletus was made of water, or air; the Earth was guarded by these elements, like an embryo in a placenta.</p><p>Popper writes that with Heraclitus, "the house catches fire"<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-4" href="#footnote-4" target="_self">4</a> . There is no longer room for stability, things are no longer things, they become <em>processes</em>, and the "apparent stability of the world is due to the laws and processes to which the world is subject".</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!by4-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fde078371-9d99-450e-89dc-9f621241e9b3_500x617.jpeg" alt="File:Heraclitus Rijksmuseum SK-A-2784.jpeg"><figcaption>Heraclitus, by Hendrick ter Brugghen (1628). Image obtained from Rijksmuseum Amsterdam. Credits <a href="https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Heraclitus_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-2784.jpeg">here</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>Of Heraclitus we know his place of origin, Ephesus, where he remained for his entire life, and some imprecise information about the dates of birth and death: it seems he was born around 535 BCE, and died around 475 BCE. Diogenes Laertius speaks of his <em>acme</em> around the LXIX Olympiad.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!sOs6!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F04c33b1d-f63c-4d47-9eeb-0ebb6d74d81c_1240x1178.png" alt=""></figure><p>At the basis of his philosophy is the concept of Logos, the word, the meaning of the world. In it everything is contained, everything contains. The unity of the world is not a rhetorical artifice: all things are <em>in reality</em> only one same thing, and we see different manifestations of it thanks to the <em>change</em> of everything into its opposite. This thought expresses the idea that the world has simultaneously plurality and unity.</p><p>An example is day and night: there is no precise moment in which there is day and not night, and then the opposite occurs; what happens is that day <em>slowly</em> changes into night, thus <em>gradually</em> becoming its opposite. And so, all things are actually equal, but appear to us different from one another. While affirming this, Heraclitus shares a thought, among the approximately 100 fragments found of his works, that "those who do not expect the unexpected will not find it, since for them it will remain hidden and incomprehensible".</p><p>If the philosophers of Miletus assumed they could trust their own senses, Heraclitus doubts it: one must use one&#8217;s senses with <em>attention</em>, since everything is in change.</p><p>Is it then useless to try to know the world and its movements? Heraclitus does not say this, on the contrary he constructs his own cosmology, <em>which has stability precisely because it is in constant change</em>: he identifies in the original material principle fire, from which the world is born, and by which the world will be consumed when the world itself ends. With change, which also occurs in the firmament, fire transforms into water, and then into earth; from this air is formed, and again fire. This cycle is the reason for the exchange of matter between sky and Earth, and is the reason for the apparent movements of the celestial bodies, which in reality do not move.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jm3L!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F76ad2549-0741-483c-aa1a-ddd0c0aeab3d_1152x1200.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>The real Magnitude of the Globe of the Earth, by Thomas Wright (1750). Image obtained from ottenuta da <a href="https://pdimagearchive.org/images/35544451-94f6-41b9-99a8-2a8592a550bd">Public Domain Image Archive / Internet Archive / The Getty</a></figcaption></figure><p>Why precisely fire? Among all the elements that are normally identified with the original principle (water, air, fire), it is precisely fire that comes closest to the concept of <em>process</em>.</p><p>Fire also generates the light that illuminates the Sun and Moon, which are fiery bowls, like the stars: the Sun and Moon are close to us, the Moon more than the Sun because it is less pure than the latter; the stars instead are very far away, and this is the reason why we do not receive their heat. The reciprocal positions of the Moon and Sun create the phenomena of eclipses.</p><p>Heraclitus enjoys posthumous fame immediately after his death, in the tradition of Plato, Aristotle and the sophists: Plato indicates Heraclitus as the author of &#960;&#940;&#957;&#964;&#945; &#8165;&#949;&#8150; (<em>p&#225;nta rhe&#238;</em>), everything flows<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-5" href="#footnote-5" target="_self">5</a>;  Aristotle considers him a monist of the Milesian school; finally the sophists, uniting the concepts of Logos, fire and God, consider him the initiator of their philosophy.</p><p>Remember the analogy with the <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/on-the-motion-of-the-earth-homer">tortuous and rough path</a> that brought us from myth to philosophy? We leave with Heraclitus a mysterious figure, ready to continue the journey.</p><p>We will immediately see how his point of view on the pervasiveness of change in the world will be overturned by another figure, who awaits us in the shadows at a crossroads between two Ways.</p><h3>Parmenides</h3><p>According to Plato<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-6" href="#footnote-6" target="_self">6</a>, Parmenides was born around 513 BCE, while Apollodorus places his birth thirty years earlier. It seems certain however that he died in 450 BCE. Born in the Greek colony of Elea from an aristocratic family, it seems he wrote the laws of his city; again Plato tells us that he was a disciple of Xenophanes, and that he had connections with Pythagorean circles.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!koWz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F91efc357-d54e-4420-9843-1dba3ef3cd1d_1240x1142.png" alt=""></figure><p>He wrote a work, titled <em>On Nature,</em> which we have almost completely thanks to Simplicius who copied it in the 6th CE from Aristotle&#8217;s <em>Physics</em>.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!EA_7!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1dfcc9fc-21c3-4c0c-8171-2279511a291a_476x1137.jpeg" alt="undefined"><figcaption>Parmenides, detail from &#8220;The School of Athens,&#8221; fresco by Raphael Sanzio(1511). Crediti <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parmenide#/media/File:Sanzio_01_Parmenides.jpg">here</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>The poem is divided into a prologue and then into two sections, one titled &#8220;the way of <em>Truth</em>,&#8221; and the other &#8220;the way of <em>mortal men</em>.&#8221; The Truth of the first way is not revealed by a goddess or muse, but is reached through deductions and logical statements that make <em>logic</em> itself <em>divine</em>, insofar as it is <em>independent</em> of men.</p><p>McKirahan speaking of Parmenides states that he "introduced deductive arguments into philosophy", and in fact his work leads one to continually ask questions like what conditions must existing things satisfy? Do our senses truly perceive reality as it is?", and his attacks on the senses and their not being suitable for searching for reality mark a fundamental watershed in the history of thought between experience and reasoning.</p><p>For Parmenides the world of change is an illusion, because change entails that something changes into something else that did not exist before: but this cannot happen, because <em>what is not cannot exist</em>. Reality exists only thanks to Being, which is uninterrupted (the void does not exist) and unchangeable (change does not exist).</p><p>Moreover, the division of his work into two ways is also the division of the world into what is objective, and what is instead subjective.</p><p>Since the only statement we can express is that a thing exists", but we cannot say that the same thing "does not exist", then the past and future, which respectively no longer exist or do not yet exist, are in reality an illusion.</p><p>If therefore even the void does not exist, the world is <em>full</em>.</p><p>The universe moreover is finite, since it cannot have a dimension that cannot be defined, and cannot be incomplete; it is spherical, that is equal in all its parts, and because it cannot be interrupted in any direction.</p><pre><code>Bright note in the margin #8

The division of the world into subjectivity and objectivity, and the personal sensation of time and space return, more than two thousand years later, in Richard Jeffries, in The Story of my Heart:
&#8220;Now is eternity. I am in the midst of it... Nothing has yet to happen: it is now. Now is eternity, now is immortal life.&#8221;</code></pre><p>Despite Parmenides&#8217; cosmology being obscure, and being described in the part of the poem dedicated to the way of Mortal Men, we can try to reconstruct it: the Earth is equidistant from every other body, and does not move; it formed from the condensation of air, while the stars form with the compression of fire, as happens for the Sun and the Milky Way; the Moon instead is formed by both air and fire<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-7" href="#footnote-7" target="_self">7</a>.</p><p>The stars are circles of fire that shine in some points rather than others; the Moon receives light from the Sun, and the Milky Way contains both hotter and more rarefied elements, and colder and denser elements<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-8" href="#footnote-8" target="_self">8</a>.</p><p>Outside the Universe there is Olympus, then Venus (which according to Diogenes Laertius was precisely Parmenides who first identified it with the Morning Star and Evening Star), then the Sun and finally the Moon. At the center of the Earth there is a <em>daimon</em> (perhaps Aphrodite, perhaps Ananke - Necessity) who generates everything and who created Eros, the first among the gods.</p><p>Parmenides sowed doubt about the possibility of knowledge: for this reason he is considered the founder of ontology in Greece, and every philosopher after him had to answer questions like (i) the Existent cannot come from the Non-Existent, since what Is cannot be, at a certain point, Not to Have Been; (ii) the Void cannot exist, because it represents what Does Not Exist; (iii) Plurality must come from Unity; (iv) motion cannot be considered a natural quality of things, it must be explained.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!enZn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6c43bdb4-8428-48c0-90a8-ee288c19fcc1_800x954.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>Illustration from a 1587 treatise on comets and meteors, created anonymously in Flanders (now northern France) and known as the <em>Kometenbuch</em> (1587). Immagine ottenuta da <a href="https://pdimagearchive.org/images/ec86edd7-7219-413b-995b-4ce65ab0c041">Public Domain Image Archive / ORKA - Open Repository Kassel</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>We will immediately see that there are those who will defend Parmenides&#8217; thesis, and those who will oppose it: but his contribution to the debate is so important that both his supporters and his detractors are defined as post-Parmenideans.</p><div><hr></div><div><hr></div><p><em><strong>In the next episode: the responses to Parmenides</strong></em></p><div><hr></div><h3>Read the other episodes:</h3><div><hr></div><p><code>Bibliography:</code></p><ul><li><p><code>Sleepwalkers: A History of Man&#8217;s Changing Vision of the Universe, by Arthur Koestler (Hutchinson 1959)</code></p></li><li><p><code>Ancient cosmologies, edited by C.
