# Homer, Hesiod, and a proto-philosopher

<h6>Disclaimer #1: questo articolo &#232; in inglese, la versione in italiano si pu&#242; leggere <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/sul-moto-della-terra-omero-esiodo">qui</a>.</h6><h6>Disclaimer #2: this episode is part of a series named &#8220;<em>on the motion of the Earth</em>&#8221;, published both on this publication, <em>Heralding Time</em>, and on <em><a href="https://lastscatteringsurface.substack.com/">Last Scattering Surface</a></em>. If you want, you can follow them both. The first chapter is <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/eng-on-the-motion-of-the-earth-sun">here</a>. The second is <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/eng-on-the-motion-of-the-earth-three">here</a>. The third <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/on-the-motion-of-the-earth-constants">here</a>.</h6><div><hr></div><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Dz4U!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd0639ebc-5e82-428a-916b-7e75cda03681_1080x1350.png" alt=""></figure><div><hr></div><p>In previous episodes we talked about the <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/eng-on-the-motion-of-the-earth-sun">first conception of the sky among ancient peoples</a>, the cosmologies of the <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/eng-on-the-motion-of-the-earth-three">Egyptians, the Babylonians, of Judaism and Islam</a>, and those developed in <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/on-the-motion-of-the-earth-constants">China, India, and Scandinavia</a>.</p><p>Starting from this episode, we will begin a long journey in search of philosophy in Greece, and why it is connected to the theme of this series. We will see that it is precisely with cosmology that the first Greek thinkers launched the philosophical tradition in Greece.</p><p>To begin the journey into Greek philosophy, we must first cross the border that separates it from mythology. Taking a look at the map, constructing the itinerary, we see that this border is not a straight line, nor even a clear boundary: it has indentations and protrusions, enclaves and unexplored territories.</p><p>The sources of Greek mythology spring from the oral tales of Minoan and Mycenaean singers between the II and I millennium BCE: stories of men and gods, heroes and muses, wars and passions, stories about the origin of places and the origin of humanity; these sources flow throughout Greece, and into the various settlements and colonies that the Greeks founded in Asia Minor and Italy, and are collected, defined, and summarised in texts presented to the public around the VIII and VII centuries BCE. The first encounter we make following this river of myths and words is with a blind man, who is perhaps more than a man, and perhaps never existed.</p><h3>A blind man who perhaps never existed</h3><p>The man we encounter is a blind bard who lived in Ionia in the VIII century before our era, and probably died in <em>Ios</em>, an island in the Cyclades in the Aegean Sea, around the middle of the following century. The man we encounter is the one to whom we attribute the two epic poems that began the Greek epic tradition, the <em>Iliad</em> and the <em>Odyssey</em>. But when questioned about how, when, and why he wrote these texts, the man vanishes.</p><p>As far as we know, Homer was never a historical figure. It is the common opinion of scholars that the two poems are not by the same author<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1" target="_self">1</a>, and that they were composed by a succession of authors and singers, who performed them publicly throughout Greece.</p><p>In any case, we can let ourselves be accompanied by the Homer of tradition, and speak of the unknown authors of the two poems as part of the figure of the legendary Homer.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!zVpO!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9c14d495-d993-4d1c-8a41-eafe7c48d208_1240x1126.png" alt=""></figure><p>The Iliad and the Odyssey contain the complete set of tools necessary to know the world, its pitfalls and dangers, its possibilities and riches. Among the stories they contain, there are also myths and stories about the creation of the world, about the shape it has and the forces that govern it.</p><p>Homer describes the sky as a <em>hemisphere</em>, a bowl turned upside down, that looms over the Earth, a flat, circular disk. Between the Earth and the sky, in the part closest to the former, there is <em>mist</em>, while in the upper part there is more rarefied air - <em>ether</em>.</p><p>Under the roots of the Earth is Tartarus, which is as far from the Earth as the Earth is from the sky, and all around the Earth is the river <em>Okeanos</em>, &#8220;which encircles the Earth and flows into itself&#8221;<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-2" href="#footnote-2" target="_self">2</a>. Okeanos is at the same time, and depending on the description, a river, a spring, an ocean, composed of fresh sweet waters, the source of all the waters of the Earth, and a salty sea. From what experience could the idea of a multiform river around the world come? Probably not from Greece, but from the traditions of the kingdoms of Egypt and Mesopotamia, which arrived in the Greek world through contacts between different populations in previous centuries.</p><p>To the west, beyond the <em>Pillars of Hercules</em>, located at the boundary between what we today call the Mediterranean Sea and the Atlantic Ocean, rises <em>Erebus</em>, the place where the souls of the dead wait to enter Tartarus.</p><p>The epic poems do not raise the question of the Earth&#8217;s motion, which is evidently stationary: this is a common experience for every member of ancient Greece. The Sun, the Moon, and the stars rise in the east, and move across the sky every day, each day falling in the west. Since they return to the east each day to rise again and again, they must somehow get back there: but Tartarus is always <em>dark</em>, and never illuminated by any light, so the celestial bodies <em>cannot cross through it</em>.</p><p>It is the task then of the river Okeanos to transport the Sun, the Moon, and the stars from west back to east, circling around the Earth. As in the Egyptian tradition, here too the celestial bodies navigate on water, after death and before being reborn.</p><p>And which stars does Homer know, which ones can men at sea use to orient themselves? Homer knows the morning star, Phosphorus, and the evening star, Hesperus; he knows the asterism of the Pleiades, that of the Hyades and the Big Dipper, and then the constellation Orion, and the stars Sirius and Arcturus.</p><p>The celestial vault is also stationary, and the motion of the celestial bodies is a reflection of the order of the world.</p><pre><code>Bright marginal note #1

- an asterism is a group of stars that forms a recognisable geometric shape, and which may or may not be part of a constellation. Famous asterisms are the Summer Triangle, formed by Altair, Deneb and Vega, or the Big Dipper, part of the constellation Ursa Major.
- the brightest stars (called the Seven Sisters) of the Pleiades asterism are also present on a bronze age metal plate, the Nebra Disk, discovered in Germany in 1999.
</code></pre><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-4T_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F39dbd62f-a4e6-4599-9420-1ec00cd5242d_1611x1584.jpeg" alt="undefined"><figcaption>Nebra Disk. The circular figure on the left could be the Sun, or the full Moon. On the right, the crescent Moon. Above between the two, the seven luminous stars of the Pleiades. Credits <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disco_di_Nebra#/media/File:Nebra_Scheibe.jpg">here</a>.</figcaption></figure><p>Homer bids us farewell, and returns to the obscurity in which his passage on Earth and through History is shrouded.</p><h3><strong>A singer of the birth of the gods</strong></h3><p>After taking leave of the figure of Homer, the journey from myth to the physical description of the world continues with the awareness that the world is full of gods: anthropomorphic, certainly, but also allegories of human concepts and emotions. To answer the question &#8220;How are the gods born?&#8221; we approach Hesiod, a singer who lived between the VIII and VII centuries BCE, and probably originally from <em>Cyme</em> (Aeolian Cyme), a city in the region of Aeolia, in Asia Minor. This man, who lived in Boeotia - a region of mainland Greece - wrote three works that we can read in their entirety: &#8220;<em>Theogony</em>,&#8221; &#8220;<em>Works and Days</em>&#8221;, and &#8220;<em>The Shield of Heracles</em>&#8221;. A poem titled &#8220;On Astronomy&#8221;<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-3" href="#footnote-3" target="_self">3</a> is also attributed to him, of which we read only fragments, which in any case do not inform us about what we wish to know.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-1-v!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6f36bea8-dc21-4a50-a50d-3c68c8ea6496_1240x1126.png" alt=""></figure><p>We are in fact gripped by the problem of the birth of the forces that govern the world, and of the gods who exercise those forces. We are freed by the song of Hesiod, who in the Theogony, the Birth of the Gods, recounts the beginning of the world and everything that follows.</p><p>The first thing that happened to the world, the first event, is the separation of Earth and Sky, into two distinct entities, since they were united. Therefore the first event is not the <em>creation</em> of something <em>nonexistent</em>, but the <em>separation</em> of something already <em>pre-existing</em>.</p><div><hr></div><div class="preformatted-block"><label class="hide-text" contenteditable="false">Text within this block will maintain its original spacing when published</label><pre class="text"><em>                                                                              [...] Verily at the first </em>Chaos<em> came to be, but next

Only later will the gods of Olympus arrive to dethrone Kronos, who in turn dethroned Ouranos, with the help of Gaia, consort of Ouranos, and Rhea, consort of Kronos.</p><p>In the beginning, however, there is <em>Chaos</em>: in the Greek tradition this term means what it also means for us, a synonym for disorder and confusion.</p><p>Here, Hesiod gives the name Chaos to that which separates Sky from Earth, the first separation. The first act of the world, therefore, is the birth of Chaos understood as the action of separation, of <em>distinction</em>.</p><pre><code>Bright marginal note #2

The conception of the distinction between Sky and Earth is present in many cultures:
- for the Maori people, Rangi (the sky) and Papa (the earth) are separated;
- <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/eng-on-the-motion-of-the-earth-three">we have seen</a> in the Egyptian myth that Nut and Geb are separated by Shu;
- the same happens in the Song of Ullikummi, a myth from Hittite-Hurrian mythology that seems to have many (subsequent) parallels in Greece;
- in the <a href="https://heraldingtime.substack.com/p/eng-on-the-motion-of-the-earth-three">Babylonian myth</a> of the Enuma Elish, the hero-god Marduk tears Tiamat into two pieces: the sky and the earth;
- Genesis: &#8220;Let there be a firmament in the midst of the waters to separate the waters from the waters.&#8221; Also due to the strong Babylonian influences on the biblical myth of the creation of the world, at a certain point the creator god of the Bible creates the firmament to separate the earth and the sky.</code></pre><p>Hesiod does not speak of what existed before this separation, nor of why it occurred. The Theogony speaks of the gods, and they are the origin of order in the world, the &#954;&#972;&#963;&#956;&#959;&#962; (cosmos). Each divinity has its own &#8220;department&#8221; to control and over which to reign; this control is not always exclusive: in some cases an event, a force, a place are contested by multiple divinities, and this creates a world that is not totally <em>ordered</em>.</p><p>The Theogony does not contain original or new ideas: it is a collection of stories and myths already present, which it undertakes to codify. But as will also happen with the first thinkers we call philosophers, Hesiod too firmly believes in an ordered world, in a divine presence in the world, which permeates both the physical and moral aspects of humanity.</p><p>Hesiod&#8217;s world is a flat, circular disk, as in Homer, and above it is the Sky, populated by the Sun, the Moon, and the stars; below it is Tartarus, never illuminated by any light. The space between sky and earth is bright by day, and dark by night. As in Homer, and as in the myths of other peoples of the Mediterranean, the celestial bodies move across the sky; after setting beyond the horizon, they navigate Ocean to return each day to the place where they will rise again, again and again.</p><p>Now Hesiod departs, and the gods he sang of remain, transforming themselves.</p><h3><strong>A syncretic and mysterious proto-philosopher</strong></h3><p>We have almost reached the end of the first stage, which from the territories of mythology arrives at the threshold of natural philosophy. The next character we encounter is a proto-philosopher, a man who transported ideas and influences from Asia to Greece, and who from Greece will bring ideas and influences back to Asia. With him we will cross the centuries straddling the eras, and the borders between kingdoms and peoples.</p><div><hr></div><p>Apollodorus of Athens maintains that Pherecydes of Syros was a contemporary of Anaximander of Miletus, and places his <em>floruit</em> - the period of greatest prominence for his contemporaries - around 540 BCE; but this is not confirmed. We know that he lived in the VI century BCE, and that a legend from the IV century BCE represents him as the teacher of Pythagoras, who in his final years would have remained with him and would have buried him<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-4" href="#footnote-4" target="_self">4</a>. We are not certain of this, but it is true that his texts circulated in Pythagorean circles, and there are similarities between the teachings of Pythagoras and Pherecydes.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-WHF!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1e891fe5-1cd3-484d-a112-8ee6065d96ef_1240x1076.png" alt=""></figure><p>Unlike the other philosophers, and adding to the strength of his legendary figure, it is said that he received no teaching from a master, but that he consulted a <em>secret book of the Phoenicians</em>, and the <em>prophecy of Ham</em>, (that is, Zoroaster). In any case, contacts with Phoenicia are evident from his father&#8217;s name, <em>Babis</em> or <em>Babys</em>, a clear name of Asian origin (probably coming from Phrygia, Pisidia or Galatia - all regions of Asia Minor).</p><p>In his work <em>The Seven Niches</em>, Pherecydes speaks of three divinities who have always existed: Zeus, whom he calls Zas, and whom he might - but it is not clear - associate with the Sun; Kronos, who although he has the same name as the god of Time dwells in and governs the Isles of the Blessed; and Chthonie, the goddess who represents the Earth.</p><p>After the union between Zas and Chthonie, the Earth becomes Ge, and she is given by Zas, now <em>also</em> become Eros, a mantle that she wears forming the earth, but which according to a subsequent passage ends up hanging on a &#8220;<em>World Tree</em>&#8221;.</p><pre><code>Bright marginal note #3

This is a story that serves two purposes: to represent the birth of the world, and to describe a human activity, the <em>anakalyptenia</em>, or the ritual gift from husband to bride that takes place on the third day of the wedding. Once again, the myths simultaneously represent what happens here on earth and what is hidden, and this and from this they influence and are influenced.</code></pre><p>Zas and Ge produce the other gods together; Chronos does so &#8220;in union with himself&#8221;: the birth of the world or a part of it as the result of an action of this type is common in Asian myths and cosmologies, and is another piece that composes the picture of Pherecydes as syncretic between Greece and Asia. We will talk about this further on.</p><p>Chronos creates fire, wind, and water from his seed, and distributes them in the world, in the five nooks of the title (&#8221;The Seven Niches&#8221; is in Greek &#7961;&#960;&#964;&#940;&#956;&#965;&#967;&#959;&#962;, and &#956;&#965;&#967;&#959;&#962; has among other possible meanings also <em>nook</em>). We do not know which places Pherecydes refers to, but they are probably the places from which the gods spring forth, the following five provinces: the sky, the Earth, Ogenos, Hades and Tartarus). The first two are obvious, while about the others we will have to speak: Ogenos is another spelling of a term we have already encountered, Okeanos. Hades and Tartarus instead are the dwelling of the dead and the place where there is no light, respectively. The evil gods defeated by Chronos, led by Ophioneus, a god whose origin can be linked to the primordial serpents of Babylonian mythologies, and who is also found in the biblical Genesis, are thrown back into the sea formed by Ogenos at the edge of the world.</p><p>In Pherecydes&#8217; cosmology, finally, the gods live on the Moon, the Earth is inhabited and all around it has Ogenos, and beneath it is the world of the dead, which in turn looms over Tartarus.</p><pre><code>Bright marginal note #4

We have spoken of Pherecydes as syncretic. Here&#8217;s why:
- Eudemus of Argos connects Pherecydes to the cosmology of Sidon, a Phoenician settlement. Here in the beginning there are Chronos (time), Pothos (wind), and Omichle (darkness). In various cosmologies of Phoenician culture, the primordial wind is represented by Aither and Aer, who generate Ul&#333;mos, which comes from the late Phoenician &#8216;&#363;l&#333;m, &#8216;&#333;l&#257;m for the Hebrews, which means Remote Time or Eternity. From these is born an egg, from which heaven and earth are generated.
- in Zoroastrian cosmology, in the beginning there is nothing but Zurvan, Time. He too generates <em>alone</em>, Ohrmazd, the Good, and Ahriman, the Evil. The first creates the material world, forming the egg-shaped firmament, in which everything is contained.
- In the sacred Indian hymns Atharvaveda, Kala (Time) autonomously generates heaven and earth.</code></pre><p>Pherecydes is a proto-philosopher, in the sense that in his work there is a principle that was missing in previous poets, and that we will encounter in those we call philosophers: the preference for prose over verse, the will to speak of the gods, even accepting their existence, <em>only</em> as an allegory of the forces that regulate the world; the description of the world as ordered <em>from the beginning</em>, without an order that arrives <em>after</em> the action of one or more gods.</p><p>From now on, even the gods will have to answer the question that philosophers pose: why? Pherecydes asks it of us too, and disappears into the Time that is already Past.</p><h3><strong>Crossing the border</strong></h3><p>We have crossed the plains of myth, and the paths of the gods. We have discovered connections, bonds, syncretisms, but also individualities, both singular and collective. It will seem in the future that we have returned to these lands; it will seem we recognize the scent of flowers, and the taste of fruits: but we will return, if we do, with a new awareness.</p><p>That myths are, have always been, and perhaps still will be, waiting for new hypotheses and theories.</p><p>The next steps will take us toward a mountain chain, in which each one represents the thought of a philosopher: these mountains have tunnels and passes that connect one to another, unique glaciers made eternal by time on unreachable peaks, streams that flow from one mountain and from another receive new water to transform into rivers of thought, lakes that collect those rivers, and the Ocean of the Earth all around, to remind us that after all, this Earth whose movement we want to understand, is Alone and One, and all around there is the Rest, and the Darkness.</p><div><hr></div><p><em><strong>In the next episode: the philosophers from Miletus.</strong></em></p><div><hr></div><h3>Read the other episodes:</h3><div><hr></div><p><code>Bibliography:</code></p><ul><li><p><code>Aristarchus of Samos, the ancient Copernicus, by Heath T.
