Anaximander’s indefinite principle and Anaximenes’ cosmic breath

Anaximander

Diogenes Laertius1 informs us that Anaximander, son of Praxiades, is the first to have built "clocks", and in the Byzantine encyclopedia (the Suda , therefore a very late source) Anaximander is spoken of as the one who invented the gnomon, a useful instrument for measuring the position of the Sun, and who “measured” the equinoxes and solstices. Furthermore, according to Diogenes he was the first to draw a map of lands and seas. The Suda also cites some writings he would have composed: “ On Nature ,” “ The Circuit of the Earth ,” “ On the Fixed Stars ,” “ The Celestial Globe ”; regarding the first Barnes speaks of a "naturalistic version of Hesiod’s Theogony"2. A legend tells that the philosopher warned the Spartans of an imminent earthquake.

Diogenes places the date of the philosopher’s death shortly after the LVIII Olympiad (547 BCE), around age 64. Apollodorus instead speaks of Anaximander born 14 years after Thales (therefore around 610 BCE) and died in the same year as his death (which occurred around 545 BCE). There is therefore a discrepancy between the two datings, but it is certain that the two philosophers were contemporary at a certain point, and it is said that Anaximander was a "student" of Thales, that is, that he heard his teachings.

Regarding the gnomon devised by Anaximander: as we saw in the previous chapter, the gnomon is a Babylonian invention, and perhaps was already known to Thales, therefore the "discovery" attributed to Anaximander can at most refer to the fact that he made use of it during his life.

Regarding the map drawn by the second philosopher of Miletus: this was indeed one of the first after Homer’s, and was perfected by Hecataeus, geographer of Miletus, following Herodotus as a source, a Greek historian of the V century BCE.

Diogenes, citing Theophrastus, states that for Anaximander the principle is the ἄπειρον ( apeiron ) which we translate as Unlimited, Indefinite, Infinite.

When he speaks of principle, Anaximander probably does not refer to the ἀρχή of which we spoke regarding Thales, cited by Aristotle: the philosopher therefore does not refer to the force that generates the world, but to the substance of which the world is initially formed.

The ἄπειρον is translatable as infinite but does not represent the spatial infinity still used today in physics: and in fact together with infinite we insert indefinite , meaning by this that this "primordial" substance lacks boundary, limit, definition.

That is, it cannot be confined, delimited, defined.

Apeiron has as its root a-peiras , that is without limit, and peir has the meaning of "to cross". It seems therefore that the term ἄπειρον represents something that is impossible to cross from side to side .

For Anaximander what is not defined has no distinction , in the sense that it is a substance that contains everything without distinction .

Aristotle speaks of this, saying that this principle is an element beyond the elements, and that it contains an infinite reserve that behaves like a spring from which to generate all the elements.

In any case, we are not sure what the interpretation was for Anaximander. It seems however, from the fragments we have3, that this Indefinite is for him the place where opposites pay "one to the other the penalty according to the order of time", and from which the same opposites originate4.

The Indefinite guides everything and "steers" everything, creation and its opposite, that is destruction , which both occur by necessity. This happens cyclically and without end, and one after another innumerable worlds are created and destroyed. The ἄπειρον is most likely in motion, otherwise how could the latter be born from something that does not contain it?

And what type of motion does it contain? It seems that it should not have any particular type, because if it had a defined one, how could all the others be born? All the different characteristics of things emerge from the ἄπειρον, each separating from its opposite, but they are not initially contained in it.

The world as we can observe it today emerges, for Anaximander, after a process of separation and differentiation of everything from its opposite .

Koestler5 speaks of Anaximander as the first who conceived a mechanistic system for the Universe. Let’s see why.

We have a fairly clear vision of his idea of the world, following various sources. According to Pseudo-Plutarch, Anaximander describes the Earth as a cylinder three times as wide as its length; Hippolytus also agrees6. According to Aristotle, the Earth for Anaximander is stationary at the center of the Universe, and is freely suspended without being supported by anything (unlike in Thales’ world, where the Earth is suspended by water), and this happens because and thanks to its “ being equally bound to all things ”7.

The firmament is spherical, and the planets and stars are wheels of fire, surrounded by a bubble of air: they contain openings, from which fire escapes, which is the source of brightness of these celestial objects.

When these openings are occluded - in what way is not said - in the Sun or Moon, it is at that moment that solar and lunar eclipses occur8.

The Moon is luminous because it contains fire like all the other stars, and does not take its brightness from the Sun.

Bright note in the margin #5

The wheels full of fire and surrounded by air present in Anaximander’s cosmology had been "seen" a few years earlier by the Hebrew prophet Ezekiel, son of Buzi, around 593 BCE in Mesopotamia:

Ezekiel 1:20: "Wherever the spirit wanted to go, they went, and the wheels rose along with them, because the spirit of the living creatures was in the wheels"

If the gods are in heaven, and in heaven there are the stars, do the gods not dwell in the stars?

The position of the celestial bodies is innovative: according to Hippolytus and Aetius, Anaximander places the fixed stars not in a sphere on the outside, but in the circle closest to Earth; then the circle of the Moon, at a distance equal to 18 times the diameter of the Earth; finally the Sun, at a distance equal to 27 times the diameter of the Earth. The Sun is as large as the Earth, and regarding the planets known at the time (Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn) we do not know if Anaximander places them together with the fixed stars or at an intermediate distance between the fixed stars and the Moon.

Description of Anaximander’s world. Illustration by D.L. Couprie in Heaven And Earth In Ancient Greek Cosmology . In the illustration, which follows that of Diels and Naddaf, the circle of stars is at nine Earth diameters, the circle of the Moon at 18, that of the Sun at 27. Credits here.

We know however that the movements in the sky all occur around the Earth, and that they are due to the wind generated by the moist exhalations of the Earth when it is heated by the heat of the Sun. It is also to the wind that the obliquity of the ecliptic is owed, that is, of the path that the Sun makes in its orbit around the Earth9.

Bright note in the margin #6

In Babylon, the numbers 3 and 9 had particular importance. In Egypt, astronomers selected 36 δεκανοί (decans), stars in the sky arranged so that their rising in the sky was distant from one another by ten degrees over periods of ten days, in order to divide the year into months.

Anaximander’s cosmology has the first circle 9 times the diameter of the Earth, the circle of the Moon 18 times that of the Earth, the circle of the Sun 27 times.

Copy of Mugler’s illustration with the description of Anaximander’s universe. The Earth is the cylinder at the center, which forms an angle with the vertical axis - the inclination of the ecliptic. The sphere is the sphere of stars, the first circle is that of the Moon, the outermost circle that of the Sun. Credits here.

Anaximander’s universe is measured in "terrestrial" dimensions: it is the first time this happens outside of a mythical tale, in which philosophy becomes slightly more quantitative and assigns a role to numbers in terms of proportion. We will see with Pythagoras that this is perhaps the foundation of Greek astronomy.

Bright note in the margin #7

The order Earth, stars, Moon, Sun is innovative for ancient Greece, but is not original in human history: it was the standard in Persian cosmology.

In the sacred texts of the Dāmdāt Nask and the Pahlavi Dēnkart one reads that beyond the Sun there is imperishable light. In Persian the expression anayra- means "without beginning", that is without ayra.

If ἀρχή is the principle, ayra also has a similar meaning.

It is strange, we said innovative earlier, the choice to place the circle of fixed stars in front of the circle of the Moon; it also seems like an idea easy to refute, in fact: how many times are the stars of the Pleiades occulted by the presence of the Moon, thus demonstrating that it is the Moon that is closer to Earth, and not the contrary?

But this decision could be due not so much to some specific astronomical observation, as to the idea that Anaximander has of the nature of these bodies. If they are composed of fire, and the "purer" the fire the higher it rises, then the brightest fire there is, and the purest - that is the Sun - will be in the highest place, followed by the Moon, and only after by the faint lights seen in the sky at night on a moonless night: the stars.

There remains the unresolved doubt about the behavior of the mist , which exhales from the Earth and creates the stars: if the Sun and Moon are visible even beyond this mist, then is its behavior such as to make partially visible and partially invisible beyond itself? This is not clear.

For Anaximander, the cause of the birth of the world already contains the forces that cause its movements. For this reason, McKirahan states that the philosopher is an ante litteram exponent of Uniformitarianism10, that is, of that doctrine born between the XVIII and XIX centuries CE according to which the geological processes that occur in the world today are the same as those that occurred in remote times.

Finally, with his idea of the Earth suspended at the center of the Universe, Anaximander allowed celestial bodies to rotate all around it, without worrying about accepting "passages" from Okeanos or visiting or not the kingdom of souls, echoes of Egyptian and Babylonian mythologies.

The Universe becomes more and more something that humanity can describe in an a-human way, becoming however increasingly intertwined with the concepts of proportion, meaning, ultimate destination - very human concepts.

Anaximenes

Anaximenes, son of Eurystratus11, is a student of Anaximander, probably also of Parmenides12. We do not know much about his life, except that his acme is around the fall of Sardis after the conquest of Cyrus the Great - which occurred around 546 BCE. He could therefore have been born around 585 BCE13. He probably died in the LXIII Olympiad, therefore between 528 and 524 BCE.

Of him we know nothing else, except that it is said he wrote at least one book.

The term air must not be misleading. The Greeks distinguish between aer and aether : the first term refers to breath, and comes from a term meaning "dark mist", and can be hot or cold; the second term instead represents crystalline air, and is the pure element that will then become the material of which the celestial spheres are filled.

Air is infinite like the ἄπειρον, but it is not indefinite . From air in fact come all the elements, thanks to a process of rarefaction and condensation: when water becomes rarefied, fire is created; when it becomes denser, it becomes wind; and then again clouds, finally water. From this, it becomes earth, and finally rock.

These changes occur because of the movement of the ἄπειρον, which is eternal.

Air, which is the πνεῦμα ( pneuma ) of the world, is the reason for its existence and its diversity: from the exhalations of air come the Sun, the Moon and the stars, but also the gods14.

Anaximenes is the first to state that the fixed stars are truly fixed , in the sense that they are bound to the celestial vault, which is crystalline because it allows one to see what is beyond.

All commentators agree in stating that Anaximenes’ Earth is flat15, that it is stationary at the center of the Universe16, and that it is supported by air; that all celestial bodies rotate around it, and are also flat " like a leaf" 17; that the Sun is in motion because of the wind generated by air, and that this movement is the reason why it is so hot; that the stars are also in motion - although they are fixed to the celestial vault , they move because the celestial vault itself is in motion - and we do not feel their heat because they are very distant; moreover, they could host other worlds like the Sun with Earth.

Unlike Anaximander, however, Anaximenes does not describe the motion of celestial bodies as a complete revolution around the Earth: when they are not visible to us, this is not due to their motion under the Earth , but to the fact that those bodies - the Sun at night, the stars by day - cross distant regions of the world that are higher than the rest of the world, and therefore invisible to observation, almost as if the table that is the Earth were also a bit oblique.

The phenomenon of eclipses is due to smaller bodies that obscure the light of the Moon or Sun.

Anaximenes explains the world by tracing everything back to the ἀρχή, and in this he unites the teachings of his two predecessors and fellow citizens: the ἀρχή is the cause of everything (the ἄπειρον for Anaximander), but it is also a visible element of the world, air (what for Thales was water).

Furthermore, he operates a reduction of multiple phenomena, from the presence of celestial bodies to the diversity of elements, to a single cause: air and its behaviours. He associates two opposite qualities - hot and cold, light and heavy, rarefied and dense - with a single quantity .

From terrestrial events like earthquakes, due according to Anaximenes to the Earth drying out and cracking18, to the gods who are born and preside over the world, everything in the Universe has air as its principle.

Traces of the Near East

We have already spoken several times about the relationship between the first Greek philosophers and Egyptian, Babylonian and in general, the mythologies of the peoples and civilisations of the east. Anaximander and Anaximenes make no exception.

Anaximander speaks of the ἄπειρον as the negation of what is limited . This Indefinite creates infinite worlds, all equidistant in time, and in which each lasts for a defined time. The world is formed with the perpetual movement of the Indefinite, first as a generative unity, which contains everything and its contrary, and then as the sphere that contains the Earth and the wheels of fire surrounded by air that are the celestial bodies. With his mechanistic vision he explains eclipses, the angulation of the ecliptic, the presence of celestial bodies.

The shape of the Earth, a cylinder wider than it is tall, is slightly concave, as if in this description there remained the echo of Babylonian, Chaldean, Iranian and Egyptian mythologies about the mountains that surround the world.

We have seen in the bright notes in the margin of this chapter that many times the ideas of Greek philosophers find a mythical origin or a religious explanation in eastern myths: the creation of Anaximander’s world is no exception, which occurs in a way similar to the Iranian one in which Ohrmazd creates the world from "what is moist and what is fiery".

Anaximenes instead speaks of air, of aer , of ἀήρ: mist, vapor. It is a visible substance, but which has the properties of the Indefinite. In relation to air as breath, in a hymn to Prâna contained in the Atharva Veda texts, it is said that everything obeys Prâna, which is breath . In the Pahlavi texts one reads that eclipses are due to smaller bodies in the sky that interpose between the world of Earth and the stars.

The three philosophers, finally

The three philosophers of Miletus, Thales, Anaximander and Anaximenes , are the first in Greece to try to discover the functioning and origin of Nature through reasoning : they observe the world, try to trace back to first principles, to generalise , to make analogies , to compare different phenomena to find their common origins .

Benjamin Farrington, historian of science and Marxist-inspired academic (Science in Antiquity, Science and Politics in the Ancient World), speaks of the philosophers of Miletus saying that they "leave out the gods" from their explanations of natural phenomena. This is certainly true insofar as what happens in the world is not the result of the will of the gods, and in which neither the creation of the world nor its end are the work of a creative or destructive divinity. Despite this, the three philosophers do not reject the existence of the gods, who are even subjected to the same process of creation as all the other things there are in the world, and who are subject to the same rules, and the same principles. Anaximander’s ἄπειρον could represent the spirit of creation of the world, but already in Anaximenes this spirit is indeed indefinite, but observable in action even on Earth.

What they seek is the general, not the particular; it is the primary cause, the universal and essential, the material cause. Now Barnes asks himself: do they do it as "scientists", in the sense we define it today?

There are three possible ways to sketch an answer: one can think that "none of the philosophers of Miletus proposed “true” theories", that "they did not compose a Royal Society [ante litteram] in Greece", and that "none of these philosophers intended to achieve the precision that is required of scientific theories today"19; in doing so, they are seen as representatives of the last poets interested in Nature, rather than the first initiators of Western science.

This vision is encouraged by the problems in the uncertain assignment of ideas and interpretations of the same due to the second, third, even fourth level sources that are the only ones we have; but also by the fact that as far as we know these philosophers did not (i) perform experiments, (ii) did not use the inductive method to build their theories nor (iii) the critical method towards each other, (iv) did not use the data at their disposal to advance hypotheses.

On the other hand there is the vision, among others proposed by Popper, according to which their works prepared the way for scientific research as we understand it, and that indeed they did the best they could do with the means at their disposal: Popper speaks in particular of Anaximander, who arrived at his idea by criticising Thales’ theory, using speculative arguments, proper to philosophy, to assert that if the Earth lies on water, one should have explained what the water rests on, and so on; and judging these infinite explanations unacceptable, he proposed a new one, which is interesting insofar as it seems to prepare for the concept of action at a distance, with his description of the Earth stationary because equally "attracted" by everything simultaneously. And it matters little if then Anaximander himself states that the Earth is flat: this is due to observational arguments, proper to science, and equally important as speculative ones.

Certainly, Thales and the other two philosophers were wrong in their ideas, but their importance, says Coleman20, does not lie in the models they proposed, rather in the fact that they began, each improving the model of the previous one, to "establish a long chain of progressively [increasingly] correct interpretation of scientific discoveries".

Finally, the third way unites the two previous ones, and presenting the three philosophers as those who allowed science as naturalistic and no longer mythical inquiry to be born, but who did not manage to adhere to the dictates that modern science considers necessary to define science as speculative and observational inquiry.

All this is not a fault that we can attribute to the three philosophers, whom we can well define - for our understanding of the terms - therefore, if not Astronomers, Cosmologists, Physicists, at least full-fledged investigators of Nature.

However, one should not think that their inquiries were homogeneous, uniform, directed by a specific idea: the concept of principle alone, for example, is not proper to the VI century BCE, but is connected a posteriori by Aristotle searching for it in the philosophers before him.

The search for the causes of movements in the sky and the origin of the world continues as a search by single individuals: who debate each other’s ideas - without undermining the theories at their core, but who do not belong to a school. Not yet.
