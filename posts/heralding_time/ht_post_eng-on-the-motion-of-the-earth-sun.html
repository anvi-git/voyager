At dawn or dusk, look for a luminous point in the sky. It won’t be flashing like an airplane, and it won’t move so quickly. It crosses the sky in just under five minutes, and it might become as bright as Jupiter or Venus.

I would be very happy if any of the readers of this series titled on the motion of the Earth were not currently on the planet, but rather on that luminous point. I don’t think this is actually happening (if I were wrong, I’d be happy to know!). If I had written this piece before October 31, 2000, I would be certain; since November 2000, however, not all humans live on Earth. Anyway, whether you inhabit the terrestrial ground or are astronauts on the ISS, we are all bound to the planet we inhabit or return to: Earth. And we have been since the dawn of human history, since when the relationship with Nature was much more intimate and direct than in contemporary society.

The sky

For a member of any human community, the relationship with Earth was expressed not only in the relationship with the soil and everything it offered, plants, trunks, trees, stones, rocks, animals, prey and predators, but also with the sky above them.

The daytime sky, dominated by the Sun and atmospheric agents, which for a long time were “celestial” objects just as much as the planets and stars; but also the Moon and planets, which sometimes visit the daytime sky, perhaps shortly before sunset or shortly after dawn.

And the nighttime sky, full of stars and illuminated by the Moon. Night comes at the end of each day, and for a member of an ancient society the stars are always there, night after night, apparently still up in the sky; observing them for a sufficient number of nights, however, one notices that they follow a path: some always remain above the horizon, others after a while are no longer visible because they die below the horizon. And the Moon has an even more easily observable cycle: in about 28 days it is a disk, then it begins to fade, becomes a crescent, and then disappears. After a few days it reappears, and it is a new Moon, regenerated, reborn, or is it always itself?

“ The sky is an instrument for ancient societies ,” writes astronomer E. C. Tubb in Echoes of the Ancient Times (1983), who used and consulted the sky to obtain a sense of cyclicity of time, to enjoy the symmetry of movements that celestial bodies painted on it, and to trust that Nature was predictable . Every civilisation, complex or simple, has devised a theory about the movements of the sky, and by reflection about the place that Earth occupies in the world, understood as we today understand the Cosmos or Universe.

The Sun

The cycles that every human can observe within one terrestrial day are the diurnal rotation of Earth, that is, the Sun moving from east to west during the day, and the stars doing likewise during the night. The word orient has its etymology in the Latin solem orientem , which means rising Sun ( orientem comes from the verb orior , which means to rise); occident, similarly, comes from the Latin solem occidentem , which means dying Sun ( occidentem comes from the verb occido , which means—among various meanings—to fall or die). For an observer who lived only one day, having risen shortly before dawn, the Sun rises in the east, fire red, travels its path in the sky during the day, shines and burns and warms the earth, then falls in the west, colors red again before disappearing, symmetrically to what had happened at dawn. Other names for east and west are from Old Norse: the origin of these names, together with those of north and south, refers to the names with which four dwarfs of Norse mythology are presented in the Poetic Edda , a collection of poems useful for knowing Norse mythology. We will talk about this.

The Night

The stars, then. Observing them day after day, for an entire year, an Egyptian priest observes and records that some stars never descend below the horizon line, that line that delimits the dome, the vault, the canvas on which the movements of the stars occur. These are the imperishable stars , and it is there that the pharaohs go after death. Today we call them circumpolar stars, since they follow a circular path in the sky, around an ideal point, called the celestial pole . In our era, the star that performs the function of pole star (in the northern hemisphere) is part of the constellation Ursa Minor , Polaris .

Top left, the constellation of Ursa Minor to which Polaris belongs. Actually, Polaris is a system of three stars, Polaris A, Polaris Ab, and Polaris B, 430 light-years away from the Solar System. The first two orbit on average at a distance of 18.5 Astronomical Units (AU) from each other, while Polaris A is 2400 AU away from Polaris B. One AU is the average distance of Earth from the Sun, about 150 million kilometers. Credits here.

The southern hemisphere does not have a star as bright as Polaris near the south pole: therefore a constellation, the Southern Cross , is used as a reference point.

The four brightest stars represent the Southern Cross, the asterism used in the southern hemisphere in the same way that Polaris is used in the northern hemisphere. Credits here.

The role of pole star is temporary: due to Earth’s movement, the position of stars in the sky changes, and in the past and future, stars different from Polaris have assumed and will assume the function of reference point for the north pole.

What is the pole for ancient civilisations? It is a cosmic axis, a real pole, raised to the sky, a tree, a mountain, a staff. Today it is an imaginary axis that represents Earth’s axis of rotation, and which intercepts the celestial sphere at two points: the north pole and the south pole.

The fourth brightest star in the sky, Arcturus , is called Marpeankurrk in Aboriginal culture, and is associated with the ancestor who had discovered the bittur , or termite larvae, considered an excellent source of protein. The bittur was available from August to the end of September, the same period when Marpeankurrk was visible. For this society, which as far as we know did not use stars to navigate at night, the stars represent ancestors, and are the mirror of things that could be found on Earth.

The first use of symbolic notation to represent the sky was found in the Dordogne region of France. Abri Blanchard , the site of the discovery, dated around 36000 BCE, contains an eagle bone on which signs are carved, which have been associated with a sequence of days: the entire sequence seems to represent a lunar cycle.

The plaque at Abri Blanchard. On the left: the signs representing the sequence associable with the lunar cycle. Credits Don Hitchcock.

During the year the position of the Sun changes, not only during the day, but day after day. There are two moments during the year when this movement seems not to exist, or to be somewhat less visible: on these days the sun seems to rise always in the same point, and so it happens at sunset. The solstice, which comes from the Latin sol sistere , represents the “ stopping of the Sun ,” and occurs around June 21, when in the northern hemisphere the Sun reaches its maximum “height” in the sky, and around December 21, when the Sun occupies the minimum “height.”

The two solstices are the reversal points of the Sun’s path in the ecliptic, which is the apparent path that the Sun takes during the year.

The ancient astronomer who observes the Moon and traces its changes, its phases , divides the year into 12 lunar cycles, called months . The word month in English has its roots in the Proto-Indo-European * meh₁- , which means to measure. In Italian, mese comes from the Latin menses , from the Greek ménē , arriving at the same root * meh₁- ; Men is a lunar divinity venerated in the Anatolian peninsula from the times of Babylonian civilisations until the Roman conquest.

The Sun and Moon are not the only objects that wander in the sky; other planets also err: planet in fact comes from the Greek plànētes astéres , wandering stars . For a Babylonian astronomer, and until the Copernican revolution, planet meant any celestial body different from fixed stars, and from comets , which were considered differently. Therefore, the Sun , Moon , Mercury , Venus , Mars , Jupiter , Saturn were planets. The other two planets of the Solar System (Uranus and Neptune) were not known, and would be observed only in the modern era. The path of the planets is close to that of the sun, in a band that composes the Zodiac .

The Chaldean astronomer, an active member of society, had the task of observing the sky and recording the position of stars and planets. Already around 3800 BCE in the kingdom of Sargon of Akkad, priest astronomers transcribed stellar accounts on clay tablets. The length of the terrestrial year was measured within 0.01% of the current correct value. From the accounts of clay tablets dated from 650 BCE to 50 BCE, we know that Babylonian astronomers steadily recorded the position of 30 stars to measure celestial coordinates. As Arthur Koestler writes in Sleepwalkers: A History of Man’s Changing Vision of the Universe , these observations were Exact Science, and allowed predictions to be made, despite the mythological character being the theoretical basis of the observations themselves.

The Gods

For a member of an ancient society, the planets had unique qualities and properties: their regular motions (the Sun and Moon), the greater brightness compared to fixed stars, the relative positions they occupied in the sky, all this placed them in a particular category.

They were gods who governed the sky, not limiting themselves to inhabiting it.

For the Egyptians, the Sun and Moon are the eyes of Horus, and the goddess of the sky, Nut, has a name that means “She who shines.” The supreme god of the Sumerians, Any (or An ) shares the symbol with which it is written with Diugir , which means “shining.” Any translates to sky , or high . In Indo-European languages Djevos means “He who governs the sky”; from this word comes Dyaus in Sanskrit, Jovis in Latin, and Zeus in Greek. Jupiter comes from Dyauspitar , or Zeuspater , which means father Zeus, and Zeus is connected to the idea of “shining,” “brilliant.” The Inti people, in the Inca region of Peru, call the Sun Tonatiuh , “ He who advances shining ”.

The Moon in Ancient Egypt is Khosnu , the runner . In Babylon it was Sin , the lord of knowledge. For the Desana people of South America the moon is the nocturnal sun.

Jupiter is associated with gods who govern the world with law: this is due to its “regular” character in the sky, as it is the planet (known to the ancients) that more than any other follows the ecliptic.

Venus, to which the cult of Ishtar in Mesopotamia is associated, is simultaneously the morning star and the evening star, since it is particularly visible at dawn and sunset. To Ishtar and consequently to Venus was associated the symbol of Bennu , a bird similar to a heron that belongs to Osiris, the god of death and resurrection. It is no coincidence that Ishtar is also the goddess of the afterlife, dying at each sunset and being born again with the following dawn.

Mercury is Set for the Egyptians, and Nebo in Babylon. To these divinities was associated the role of messenger of the gods, and probably this role was due to the behavior of the planet, observed year after year: it is the fastest planet to travel from west to east along the ecliptic, to overtake the Sun and return in the opposite direction.

Mars is visible to the naked eye as a planet redder than others: this would be enough to associate its color with blood, and therefore with war. In Babylon they associated it with Nergal , god of war; so also the analogous Greek god Ares and the Roman god Mars .

Saturn was Ninib for the Babylonians, Horus for the Egyptians and Cronos for the Greeks.

Stars were also associated with divinities, sometimes minor ones. For the Aztecs the warrior god Tezcatlipoca was associated with Ursa Major; however, it did not represent a bear, but rather the warrior whose name means Smoking Mirror: the god was represented with the left foot missing, because during the year a part of the constellation of Ursa descends below the horizon, and is not entirely visible. Even the gods are subject to the limits of our observations.

Every civilisation has investigated the skies, then: whether they are seen as the dwelling of gods or ancestors, or places of symmetry and regularity, or all these things together. A. Koestler states that “ Science emerges in the form of Janus ,” that is, it simultaneously contains the objective rigor of scientific research and the personal desire to connect Nature to myth. In the next episodes we will see that every astronomical theory about the nature of the world and the motion of Earth rests on the knowledge and thought of the time in which it was formulated, but also on all the theories that preceded it, whether they are in agreement or disagreement with the theory itself.

Astronomy has as many faces as there are skies it scrutinizes: countless, yet united in a single vault.
