It won’t be flashing like an airplane, and it won’t move so quickly. It crosses the sky in just under five minutes, and it might become as bright as Jupiter or Venus.</p><p>I would be very happy if any of the readers of this series titled <strong>on the motion of the Earth</strong> were not currently on the planet, but rather on that luminous point. I don’t think this is actually happening (if I were wrong, I’d be happy to know!). If I had written this piece before October 31, 2000, I would be certain; since November 2000, however, <a href="https://www.nasa.gov/international-space-station/space-station-facts-and-figures/#:~:text=The%20space%20station%20has%20been,station%20during%20a%20crew%20handover.">not all humans live on Earth</a>. Anyway, whether you inhabit the terrestrial ground or are astronauts on the ISS, we are all bound to the planet we inhabit or return to: Earth. And we have been since the dawn of human history, since when the relationship with Nature was much more intimate and direct than in contemporary society.</p><h3>The sky</h3><p>For a member of any human community, the relationship with Earth was expressed not only in the relationship with the soil and everything it offered, plants, trunks, trees, stones, rocks, animals, prey and predators, but also with the sky above them.</p><p>The daytime sky, dominated by the Sun and atmospheric agents, which for a long time were “celestial” objects just as much as the planets and stars; but also the Moon and planets, which sometimes visit the daytime sky, perhaps shortly before sunset or shortly after dawn.</p><p>And the nighttime sky, full of stars and illuminated by the Moon. Night comes at the end of each day, and for a member of an ancient society the stars are always there, night after night, apparently still up in the sky; observing them for a sufficient number of nights, however, one notices that they follow a path: some always remain above the horizon, others after a while are no longer visible because they die below the horizon. And the Moon has an even more easily observable cycle: in about 28 days it is a disk, then it begins to fade, becomes a crescent, and then disappears. After a few days it reappears, and it is a new Moon, regenerated, reborn, or is it always itself?</p><p>“<em>The sky is an instrument for ancient societies</em>,” writes astronomer E. C. Tubb in Echoes of the Ancient Times (1983), who used and consulted the sky to obtain a sense of <em>cyclicity</em> of time, to enjoy the <em>symmetry</em> of movements that celestial bodies painted on it, and to trust that Nature was <em>predictable</em>. Every civilisation, complex or simple, has devised a theory about the movements of the sky, and by reflection about the place that Earth occupies in the world, understood as we today understand the Cosmos or Universe.</p><div><hr></div><h3>The Sun</h3><p>The cycles that every human can observe within one terrestrial day are the diurnal rotation of Earth, that is, the Sun moving from east to west during the day, and the stars doing likewise during the night. The word <em>orient</em> has its etymology in the Latin <em>solem orientem</em>, which means <em>rising Sun</em> (<em>orientem</em> comes from the verb <em>orior</em>, which means to rise); <em>occident,</em> similarly, comes from the Latin <em>solem occidentem</em>, which means dying Sun (<em>occidentem</em> comes from the verb <em>occido</em>, which means—among various meanings—to fall or die). For an observer who lived only one day, having risen shortly before dawn, the Sun rises in the east, fire red, travels its path in the sky during the day, shines and burns and warms the earth, then falls in the west, colors red again before disappearing, symmetrically to what had happened at dawn. Other names for east and west are from Old Norse: the origin of these names, together with those of north and south, refers to the names with which four dwarfs of Norse mythology are presented in the <em>Poetic Edda</em>, a collection of poems useful for knowing Norse mythology. We will talk about this.</p><div><hr></div><h2>The Night</h2><p>The stars, then. Observing them day after day, for an entire year, an Egyptian priest observes and records that some stars never descend below the horizon line, that line that delimits the dome, the vault, the canvas on which the movements of the stars occur. These are the <em>imperishable stars</em>, and it is there that the pharaohs go after death. Today we call them <em>circumpolar</em> stars, since they follow a circular path in the sky, around an ideal point, called the <em>celestial pole</em>. In our era, the star that performs the function of <em>pole star</em> (in the northern hemisphere) is part of the constellation <em>Ursa Minor</em>, <em>Polaris</em>.</p><div There are two moments during the year when this movement seems not to exist, or to be somewhat less visible: on these days the sun seems to rise always in the same point, and so it happens at sunset. The solstice, which comes from the Latin <em>sol sistere</em>, represents the “<em>stopping of the Sun</em>,” and occurs around June 21, when in the northern hemisphere the Sun reaches its maximum The word month in English has its roots in the Proto-Indo-European *<em>meh₁-</em>, which means to measure. In Italian, <em>mese</em> comes from the Latin <em>menses</em>, from the Greek <em>ménē</em>, arriving at the same root *<em>meh₁-</em>; <em>Men</em> is a lunar divinity venerated in the Anatolian peninsula from the times of Babylonian civilisations until the Roman conquest.</p><div><hr></div><p>The Sun and Moon are not the only objects that wander in the sky; other planets also err: planet in fact comes from the Greek <em>plànētes astéres</em>, <em>wandering stars</em>. For a Babylonian astronomer, and until the Copernican revolution, planet meant any celestial body different from <em>fixed</em> stars, and from <em>comets</em>, which were considered differently. Therefore, the <em>Sun</em>, <em>Moon</em>, <em>Mercury</em>, <em>Venus</em>, <em>Mars</em>, <em>Jupiter</em>, <em>Saturn</em> were planets. The other two planets of the Solar System (Uranus and Neptune) were not known, and would be observed only in the modern era. The path of the planets is close to that of the sun, in a band that composes the <em>Zodiac</em>.</p><p>The Chaldean astronomer, an active member of society, had the task of observing the sky and recording the position of stars and planets. Already around 3800 BCE in the kingdom of Sargon of Akkad, priest astronomers transcribed stellar accounts on clay tablets. The length of the terrestrial year was measured within 0.01% of the current correct value. From the accounts of clay tablets dated from 650 BCE to 50 BCE, we know that Babylonian astronomers steadily recorded the position of 30 stars to measure celestial coordinates. As <em>Arthur Koestler</em> writes in <em>Sleepwalkers: A History of Man’s Changing Vision of the Universe</em>, these observations were Exact Science, and allowed predictions to be made, despite the mythological character being the theoretical basis of the observations themselves.</p><div><hr></div><h3>The Gods</h3><p>For a member of an ancient society, the planets had unique qualities and properties: their regular motions (the Sun and Moon), the greater brightness compared to fixed stars, the relative positions they occupied in the sky, all this placed them in a particular category.</p><p>They were gods who governed the sky, not limiting themselves to inhabiting it.</p><p>For the Egyptians, the Sun and Moon are the <em>eyes</em> of Horus, and the goddess of the sky, Nut, has a name that means “She who shines.” The supreme god of the Sumerians, <em>Any</em> (or <em>An</em>) shares the symbol with which it is written with <em>Diugir</em>, which means “shining.” Any translates to <em>sky</em>, or <em>high</em>. In Indo-European languages <em>Djevos</em> means “He who governs the sky”; from this word comes <em>Dyaus</em> in Sanskrit, <em>Jovis</em> in Latin, and <em>Zeus</em> in Greek. <em>Jupiter</em> comes from <em>Dyauspitar</em>, or <em>Zeuspater</em>, which means father Zeus, and Zeus is connected to the idea of “shining,” “brilliant.” The Inti people, in the Inca region of Peru, call the Sun <em>Tonatiuh</em>, “<em>He who advances shining</em>”.</p><p>The Moon in Ancient Egypt is <em>Khosnu</em>, the <em>runner</em>. In Babylon it was <em>Sin</em>, the lord of knowledge. For the Desana people of South America the moon is the nocturnal sun.</p><p>Jupiter is associated with gods who govern the world with law: this is due to its “regular” character in the sky, as it is the planet (known to the ancients) that more than any other follows the ecliptic.</p><p>Venus, to which the cult of <em>Ishtar</em> in Mesopotamia is associated, is simultaneously the morning star and the evening star, since it is particularly visible at dawn and sunset. To Ishtar and consequently to Venus was associated the symbol of <em>Bennu</em>, a bird similar to a heron that belongs to Osiris, the god of death and resurrection. It is no coincidence that Ishtar is also the goddess of the afterlife, dying at each sunset and being born again with the following dawn.</p><p>Mercury is <em>Set</em> for the Egyptians, and <em>Nebo</em> in Babylon. To these divinities was associated the role of messenger of the gods, and probably this role was due to the behavior of the planet, observed year after year: it is the fastest planet to travel from west to east along the ecliptic, to overtake the Sun and return in the opposite direction.</p><p>Mars is visible to the naked eye as a planet redder than others: this would be enough to associate its color with blood, and therefore with war. In Babylon they associated it with <em>Nergal</em>, god of war; so also the analogous Greek god <em>Ares</em> and the Roman god <em>Mars</em>.</p><p>Saturn was <em>Ninib</em> for the Babylonians, <em>Horus</em> for the Egyptians and <em>Cronos</em> for the Greeks.</p><p>Stars were also associated with divinities, sometimes minor ones. For the Aztecs the warrior god <em>Tezcatlipoca</em> was associated with Ursa Major; however, it did not represent a bear, but rather the warrior whose name means Smoking Mirror: the god was represented with the left foot missing, because during the year a part of the constellation of Ursa descends below the horizon, and is not entirely visible. Even the gods are subject to the limits of our observations.</p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank"
