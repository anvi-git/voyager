In the first episode, Egyptian priests, Babylonian astronomers, gods and warriors appeared to populate the sky, with order governed by shining divinities.

In this episode, we encounter the civilisations of Asia between the Mediterranean, the Red Sea, and the Persian Gulf: Egypt and the river, Mesopotamia and the rivers, the Hebrew world and the Arab world with the one creator god.

For the civilisations of Mesopotamia, the world is circular; for the kingdom of Egypt, a rectangular box: for the peoples of these kingdoms, the world could be anything they imagined, says A. Koestler, whom we already met in the first episode.

The objects in the sky have paths that are overall comprehensible, repetitive, cyclical, and quite predictable. One could then seek meaning in all this, and we have already seen that this is indeed what humanity has always tried to do: seek a celestial order that reflected the desired social order, or be influenced by it to build one on Earth that mirrored it.

The Nile within me, and the Milky Way above me

Cosmology and astronomy in Egypt span the over three thousand years of the country’s history (~3000 BCE - ~30 CE) as an independent kingdom, continuously transforming and adding ideas and suggestions: if the sky represents the order of the world, and pharaohs live in the sky after death, then what changes on Earth will be reflected in the sky. Furthermore, the kingdom is vast enough to allow different cosmological streams to flourish in as many cities.

First of all, the Egyptians are (almost never) disturbed by neighbouring populations. The Nile valley is the privileged direction toward which an Egyptian pharaoh looks to administer the kingdom; he doesn’t need to worry about anything else; the Mediterranean Sea is a border. The kingdom is desert and oasis, and all around there are mountains that enclose and protect the country; the mountains are so important as to define the natural border: “foreign country” has as its symbol a stylised mountain range, 𓈉.

The kingdom extends to the “Gate of the South,” near Elephantine, Cebu or Abu for the Egyptians, in the Aswan region. At the four corners of the kingdom are four mountains that support the sky. The two boundaries of the North-South axis are water boundaries, and water is also everything that surrounds the world: the Great Circuit , or the Great Circular Ocean contain the world, which the Nile divides in two. The water of the Ocean, the Nile, and rain all come from the first of the gods, Num .

The Milky Way is the river in the sky, like the Nile on Earth.

The Sun and Moon move in the sky, and when the Sun sets, death arrives, until a new dawn, which is the moment when the Sun, after traveling under the Earth, returns to illuminate Egypt. The Sun lives a human life every day, and the Moon is also alive and changeable, changing shape during the month. The Sun is the god Râ , and the Moon Yââhu Aûhû , and sometimes they are attacked by a serpent: when this happens, eclipses occur. The stars move in the sky, and sometimes disappear. Some are linked to what happens on Earth: Sirius is visible in the days preceding the annual flooding of the Nile. The five known planets were Bonû , which at sunset was Ûati and at dawn Tiû-nûtiri , or “the god who greets the Sun before its arrival” (Venus), Sobkû (Mercury), Doshiri (Mars), Ûapshetatûi (Jupiter), and Kahiri (Saturn).

In the beginning there were primordial waters, and a primordial abyss of waters: “ in infinity, nothingness, nowhere, and darkness .” After this introduction, and the idea that the world is governed by Ma’at, that is, by order, personified by a goddess with a headdress of ostrich feathers, cosmogonies differ depending on the place where they are born. For an Egyptian astronomer, the stories of the world come from Khnum (Hermopolis), Iunu (Heliopolis), and Men-Nefer (Memphis).

Khnum

The Greek name comes from the divinity of wisdom, which for the Egyptians was Thot, and which the Greeks associated with Hermes Trismegistus, but Khnum translates as “the eighth city.” The genies of the world were Nau and Naunet , lords of what is deep; Huh and Hauhet , gods of what has no limit; Kuk and Kakwet , guardians of what is darkness; and Amun and Amaunet , sovereigns of what is invisible. These genies formed the primordial egg of Num, from which light and air emerged. In Thebes it is told that this egg was produced by a goose symbol of the Great Primordial Spirit, Kenken Wer , which was the first being to produce a sound (Kenken Wer can be translated as “he who cackles”).

In another version of this story, the waters came from a lotus flower from which an infant was born, the Sun. At dawn the Sun is born from the lotus flower, and returns to it after sunset, when the flower closes its petals.

Iunu

The Greek name means “city of the sun,” and Iunu means “the Pillars.” Here the god is Atum, “He who is Complete,” who emerged from the primordial waters in the form of a hill (according to other sources, sitting or standing on a hill). For some, he is the son of Nun . He represents the small elevations of terrain that form when water retreats after Nile floods. The hill, the symbol of this divinity, is remembered by the Egyptians in the construction of pyramids, to symbolise the Primordial Hill.

In Utterance #600, one of the Pyramid Texts, that is, the oldest religious utterance texts found of Egyptian civilisation, dating around 2400 BCE, it is told that Atum made the world starting from himself (in his image?). He created Shu and Tefenet by spitting. This action designates a characteristic of Egyptian philosophy, for which evoking the name of something corresponds to creating that something. As testimony to this, Shu and Tefenet originate in ishish , a verb that also translates as “to spit.” Shu represents air, and Tefenet humidity. The first a masculine divinity, and the second feminine, together they generate Geb , the god of earth, and Nut , the goddess of sky. The generation of green Geb and blue Nut occurs when Shu separates the two siblings from their embrace.

In another story, Nut marries Geb, but the sun-god Râ disagrees: for this reason he separates them, cursing them never to be able to conceive offspring in the future on any day of the year. Yet another story tells that the god Thoth , taking pity on Nut, managed to win from the Moon, after playing the game of checkers, 1/70 of the planet’s light time. He thus creates five additional days, to add to the Egyptian year; and it is in these additional days that Nut conceives Osiris , Horus , Set , Isis , and Nephthys . The Egyptian year was until that moment 360 days: it thus becomes the astronomical year of 365 days.

Water surrounds the world; before the world there was only water. Humanity also comes from water: the word for humanity, romet , comes from remeyet, the tears of Neb-er-Djer , the Universal Lord.

Men-Nefer

At Khnum it is told that Atum has no consort, and that he generated his own children “with his own hand”; at Men-Nefer the Primordial Hill is Ta-tjenen , the land that rises.

Men-Nefer has as god Ptah, who generates Nun and Nuret. The old gods are also contained in Ptah and generated by him. The creation of the world occurs through the heart of Ptah, seat of intelligence, from which Horus is born, and from his tongue, from which descends command and the power of the word, which gave rise to Thoth.

Once the creation of the world has occurred, and humanity has populated the Earth, the order of the world must be maintained as it has always been. The chaos that is exiled to the borders of the world must be kept there, and it is the actions of man and the gods that allow it. A balance, therefore: we return to the concept of Ma’at, to cosmic order, to earthly order, finally to ethics. In the world it represents truth and justice. Every day must be preserved, from its creation to its departure with night; the pharaoh reigns for this, and his temples are a testimony to it.

Between the Tigris and Euphrates, the Earth is Flat

The region of Mesopotamia is inseparably linked to the fertile plains bordering the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, and its extension today would cover Iraq, Iran, Syria, and Turkey. The civilisation we will discuss, and the cuneiform texts that still speak to us today, are dated between 3000 BCE and 300 BCE. Cosmological ideas come from texts of the first millennium BCE.

The region of Mesopotamia. In addition to the two rivers Tigris and Euphrates, some important cities of the region are also highlighted.

The region, so fertile and crossed by the rivers we have discussed, was at the center of the world for a Sumerian farmer, or a priest of Babylon, or an Assyrian warrior. The Babylonian story of the creation of the world, Enuma Elish , speaks of a world born from liquid chaos. In the beginning there is no sky, but only fresh waters, Apsu , and salt waters, Ti’amat .

The presence of water as a creative element is common to sources in Mesopotamia, and therefore in Syria, but also in Egypt, Palestine, and Egypt. The presence of such fertile land, where vegetation grows spontaneously, does not lead the creator of myths to imagine a paradise in the sky that produces what humanity needs through rain, precisely because plants grow alongside the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. These rivers had their sources in underground springs, primordial springs created by Apsu, the master god, called Enki in Sumerian and Ea in Babylon.

After Apsu and Ti’amat come Lahmu and Lahamu , who form the horizon with silt. The sky is the dwelling of Anshar , from whom Anu is born, paradise; under the earth dwells Kishar , from whom Nudimut is born, the earth. Anu and Nudimut are imprisoned by Ti’amat, but are freed by Marduk , descendant of Kishar and Anshar, when he cuts Ti’amat in two in her dwelling, separating them. Thus the sky and earth are formed. In some texts, the earth is Nippur , and Eridu is water.

Cosmology in Mesopotamia describes the Earth at the center, the sky and celestial bodies above it, and cosmic waters and the afterlife below it. The stars are fixed, the planets move.

The Earth is a disk - although in the northern region the Earth is instead “a mountain”; with the extension of the kingdom, it becomes a large disk, but of limited extension, due to the motion of the Sun and other celestial bodies. The Sun travels under the Earth every night; so does the Moon. The extension of the Earth is surrounded by a Khubur , Salt River, beyond which there are (8?) “ triangular regions .”

Babylonian astronomy reached a dead end; the problem lies, according to Lambert, in the fact that it was not easily extendable and generalizable: one example above all, the presence of a flat Earth instead of a spherical one. Most of the important contributions we receive from cosmology coming from this area come from Chaldean astronomy, which we will discuss briefly when we talk about Greek philosophers. It is from here indeed that many geometric and astronomical theories developed by the Greeks come.

Creatio ex nihilo?

The Hebrew cosmological tradition is as ancient as its people, and extends to the beginning of the Late Middle Ages. One thing that is evident is that Hebrew culture adopted the cosmological framework of the civilisation in which it lived.

If for the Babylonians the Earth is flat, then the biblical narrative is also consistent with this description: the Earth is a flat disk, and at the end of the world there are four kanefot , corners. Above the Earth there is shamayin , the sky, and rakia, the firmament. The Earth lies on pillars, and the firmament has an end. The Sun and Moon move in the firmament, or under it. Under the Earth there is Sheol , the dwelling of the dead. Waters (remember the Babylonians?) live both above the firmament and under the Earth; the latter formed the seas and oceans at the moment of creation. In the depths of the Earth there is Tehom (doesn’t it remind you of Ti’amat?), the great depth, from which the waters of the flood come.

The creation of the world occurs thanks to God, the Creator; the root of the verb bara, to create, does not mean creatio ex nihilo , creation from nothing, but creation “out of something (or someone).” Only later will the concept of creation from nothing arrive (in the Books of Maccabees, around the I-II century BCE).

In rabbinic literature, cosmic speculation is not encouraged; nevertheless, various theories about the nature of the world are born. Perhaps God created the world we inhabit after having created and destroyed many others; perhaps the Sun and Moon had the same size, but the Moon, protesting that it wanted to be larger, was made smaller.

The distance from Earth to the firmament is a unit of time, 500 years. If there are three firmaments, each will be 500 years away from the other. The Creator has 12 constellations for each host, and for each 30 legions, for each of which 30 cohorts, and again 30 maniples, and all have each 30 camps, and in each camp 365 thousand stars, a thousand for each day of the year.

How long will the Universe last? 6000 years, then it will be desolate for the next thousand, or 2000.

As we have said, Hebrew cosmology is influenced by the peoples with whom the Jews come into contact; in the 12th century BCE, Maimonides develops a cosmology starting from the neo-Platonic conception of the world, with nine spheres, for the Moon, Mercury, Venus, the Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, the stars, and the zodiac. In this vision, the spheres rotate around the Earth from East to West, and the color of the sky is an optical illusion; the spheres are transparent, without weight, without color. The Earth has a surface 40 times the surface of the Moon, and the Sun has a surface 170 times the surface of the Earth.

One Universe for One God

Islamic cosmology is the set of Aristotelian and Ptolemaic theories, filtered through neo-Platonism and hermeticism, with characteristics of Gnostic worship. Thus summarises the source we used for this section, in which we speak of the vision of Islamic scholars.

The starting point is the creation of the Universe by the Creator God. There is no agreement, as in the Hebrew tradition, whether this occurred starting from the existing, or whether creation was ex nihilo . God created everything that is, and separated heaven from Earth, because before they were united. Heaven and Earth are formed respectively by seven heavens and seven Earths: this explains the presence of seven different climatic zones; and of the seven layers of Hell; above the layers of heaven there is a footstool, on which sits the throne of God. The Earth and the sub-lunar region are the place where minerals, plants, animals reside, formed by fire, air, water, and earth.

The celestial spheres host, in order of proximity from Earth: the Moon, Mercury, Venus, the Sun, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, the fixed stars divided into the 12 regions of the zodiac, a ninth sphere. Called the “Encompassing Sphere” by Thabit ibn Kuwa , this sphere serves to explain the equinoxes.

Despite observations disproving the presence of circular motions for planets, the geometric instruments of epicycles, eccentrics, and equants are used to save the phenomena : how often this has been done in history, we will see soon! Muslim scholars are divided between supporters of Ptolemy , like Ibn al-Hatham , who studies planetary motion with homocentric spheres, and the theory of solid spheres, because the void does not exist; and the Islamic Peripatetics, who critics of Ptolemy publish “A Correction to the Almagest,” they too however using the theory of homocentric spheres (which we will discuss when we meet the Greeks in this story).

The love for hypotheses and reasoning leads scholars to wonder if other types of motion could work: if for example, in addition to circular revolutionary motion, there could not be elliptical motions. In any case, as al-Biruni says, this type of debate is mainly intellectual, and the question of the nature of motions in the sky is a physical question, not astronomical. For these and other reasons, including a growing departure from philosophical rationality in favor of irrational mysticism, and reasons of political and religious order, from the X to the XVI century also in the Islamic world the cosmological debate stagnates, while it is slowly rediscovered in medieval Europe. But we will talk about this later.
