In previous episodes we talked about humanity’s first steps under the sky, the Sun, the planets and the stars, and the cosmologies of the Egyptians, the Babylonians, the Hebrews and the Islamic world.

In this episode we’ll talk about three other cultures that built a cosmology describing the place of Earth in the world: China, India, and Scandinavia.

China

In China’s millennia-long history, the first concepts of astronomy and the first cosmological conceptions arrive with the kai thien school of thought, which is succeeded by the hsuan yen , to finally arrive at the hun thien , which will supplant the other two.

Chinese astronomers recognize the ecliptic, perform measurements of celestial events, from comets to supernovae to solar or lunar eclipses; however, the lack of a deductive geometry like the one that will develop in Greece prevents the development of celestial mechanics.

Kai thien

or “celestial cover,” represents a vaulted universe. The oldest school of those we’ll discuss, formed according to tradition during the time of the Shang dynasty (around 2000 BCE), describes the sky as a square-shaped bowl, upon which sits a circular hemisphere representing the sky. Sky and Earth are 80 thousand li apart (about 40 thousand kilometers).

Earth is surrounded at its borders by a “border ocean,” into which the rains flow. The celestial sphere rotates around Earth from right to left, and the Sun and Moon, bound to this movement, also have their own proper motion. The setting and rising of the Moon and Sun are illusions, however, like the circumpolar stars: they’re no longer visible because they’ve moved too far away.

Hsuan yen

stands for “infinite vacuity,” or infinite empty space. This doctrine describes the sky, the firmament, as completely empty and devoid of substance. The sky is immensely distant, immensely high, it has no limit whatsoever. Celestial bodies like the Sun and Moon are nothing but condensed vapor, and they wander through empty space, probably moving, or standing still. They’re not bound to one another, and fixed too is the star that determines the North Pole. The Big Dipper too, the constellation useful for finding North, never falls below the horizon.

Hun thien

or “celestial circles,” is the doctrine that later replaced the two more ancient doctrines. The sky is like a chicken egg, and is spherical like a crossbow bolt. Earth is like the egg yolk, and lies at its center, at the center of the Universe. In the lower parts of the sky there is water, upon which Earth rests, and the heavens rest upon vapour.

The center of Earth is traversed by a celestial axis around which the sky rotates; this axis was initially located in China, which is the center of the world, but now it is no longer: it’s the result of a battle between the monster Kung Kung and China’s fourth mythical emperor, Yao , who shifted the sky in their battle, thus also changing the position of the world’s rotation axis.

This theory became around 180 CE the theory considered “true” in China, since as Chang Heng , a Chinese astronomer of the 1st century CE, formulates it, “there is no one alive currently who promotes this theory [hsuan yeh],” and “[kai thien] has been proven to be wrong and lacking many proofs and experiments.”

Accumulation, not synthesis

Indian cosmology, which R.F. Gombrich emphasises is different from Indian astronomy, can be divided into four strands, which follow the different doctrines that have visited the Asian continent over the millennia.

Vedic and Indu, Buddhist and Jain

The Purāṇas , among the first Hindu sacred texts, contain two themes linked to cosmology: the universe in space and the universe in time, and contain countless theories about human history, linked to different origins of the world. The complexity of these texts is due to the necessity and will to add descriptions, origins and explanations to already existing texts, rather than operating a synthesis of all previous knowledge.

In the Rig Veda , a collection of sacred texts dated approximately to the second half of the II millennium BCE, the universe is divided between earth, feminine, and sky, masculine, and the elements that compose it are earth, atmosphere, and sky; the universe begins with fire contained in primordial water.

In the sacred Brahmanas texts, the world is a shell divided in half, formed by two materials and two parts: gold is the sky, and silver represents Earth. The distance of earth from sky is “ 1000 days of travel by horse, and equals the height reached by 1000 cows arranged one on top of another .”

From the Inquiry of Suka , a text describing the creation of time, we know that from Brahma, the great being, originates thought that creates the elements: ether, wind, fire, water and finally earth.

Earth is a mass at the center of the Universe; it’s a flat disc, divided from being a single mass by oceans and continents. At the center of Earth is Mount Meru, a divine mountain located at the center of the world.

For the Jains, time is eternal, and Earth is a disc. In Buddhist philosophy, space and time are infinite; and infinite is the number of Earths.

Earth rests upon water, and water upon air, and air upon space. The world, that is the Universe, expands and contracts.

“Edo,” or “I write”

The cosmology of Scandinavian peoples originates in the time of pagan worship, and can be traced back to the Scandinavian Bronze Age (2000 - 500 BCE). It arises from oral culture, and its main text is the Prose Edda (to which is added the Poetic Edda ) compiled by Icelandic historian Snorri Sturluson in the XIII century CE.

At the center of the world, which is a flat disc, there is a tree, and all around Earth there is an ocean, which joins with underground primordial waters. Under the roots of the tree at the center of the world lies an enormous serpent. The tree’s name is Yggdrasil , and before the beginning of time there was only a formless abyss, Ginnuugagap . Life emerged from ice, and the first giant was named Ymir , which means being with two natures, because he was formed by spark and embers.

Three legendary men kill the giant and form the world: from his body the Earth, from his blood the ocean, from his skin the sky. The sky is supported at the four corners of the world by four dwarves: Austri , Vestri , Nordri , and Sudri . East, West, North, South.

The gods of Scandinavian mythology are themselves representations of celestial powers, and the main ones are Thor and Odin , who are Aesir , and Freyr and Freyja , who are Vanir . The Aesir (or Asi) are the warrior deities, the Vanir are instead the deities linked to meteorological and natural events.

Once again, the sky and what happens there find explanation on Earth, and serve a purpose.

If in a kingdom order must remain as such, everything stays fixed, as in heaven, so on Earth; a land that is instead constantly invaded by other peoples, whose order is continuously modified and indeed overturned, tends to transport this dynamism into a cosmology that proceeds by accumulation and not by synthesis; again, an oral culture that recognises itself in a couple of works that have the purpose of summarising and making them consistent from beginning to end will create with those works an encyclopedia of previous myths and theories.
