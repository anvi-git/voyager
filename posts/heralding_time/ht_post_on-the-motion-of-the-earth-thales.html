Welcome back.

In previous episodes, ancient skies full of gods appeared, rivers and mountains in Egypt and Babylon, upturned bowls and sacred texts in China, India and Scandinavia.

Having returned to Asia Minor, we have traveled the border between mythology and cosmology in Greece. Having left behind a blind poet, a narrator of the birth of the gods, and a syncretic proto-philosopher, it is time to talk about the first philosophers who appeared in Greece, and the worlds they described and the place of the Earth in them.

Too many questions?

Before setting off, let’s try to clarify the reason for the title of this series: why, to understand what the answers have been to an apparently technical question like “what is the motion of the Earth?”, have we talked about a bronze age artifact , divinities and sacred texts, to arrive in Greece to talk about philosophy?

Because the Greek philosophers are the first to try to answer this question in a way similar to how we do, posing questions valid in every time, and they respond by questioning Nature, no longer the gods. The two interpretations of the world can indeed coexist - not all philosophers reject the concept of the existence of gods, obviously - but philosophy in Greece proceeds in the direction of “filling the gaps” of inexplicable natural events with logical, rational explanations, even if not necessarily “scientific”: the concept of scientific experiment as we understand it today is not born in the classical Greek world, but in some cases one can glimpse an attitude that we could define as “replicable” or “quantifiable”.

The questions that the Greek philosophers ask themselves, from Miletus to Athens, from Croton to Samos, concern the world, now conceived as a unity , autonomous unto itself; the material constitution of the world itself, continuous unity or formed by discrete elements; its birth or creation as the product of an accident , a design or a necessity ; the possibility that its Creator is a transcendent or immanent figure; its duration, whether finite or endless ; the number of worlds, one or innumerable , and in this case whether contemporary or successive to one another.

These and other questions bounce from island to island, from city to city, transported by the lives of these thinkers, and by the concept common to all of them, that ideas are discussed and do not constitute dogma .

Arthur Koestler, whom we have already encountered in previous episodes, speaks of the 6th century BCE as a period in which humanity awakened and began to seek answers to the questions we still ask ourselves today: he speaks of figures like Buddha, Confucius, Lao-Tze, and then of Ionian philosophers and Pythagoreans.

The first three are mystical, religious figures, of authority and not men of science, however wise and fundamental in morality and philosophy.

We will now speak of the philosophers of Miletus - the Ionians of whom Koestler speaks, the philosophers of Nature, starting with Thales.

But first, let’s talk about Miletus.

Miletus

The polis of Miletus exists, at the time of the philosophers we will discuss in this episode, for more than 300 years. Like other cities of the Aegean Sea islands, it was founded after the collapse of the Mycenaean civilization of the bronze age.

Starting from the dall’VIII century BCE these cities create colonies in Southern Italy, to the west, and on the coasts of the Black Sea, to the north. Especially in this way, and with the settlements in Asia Minor, the citizens of the poleis come into contact with other populations, coming from Mesopotamia, or from Egypt, or from Asia Minor itself.

Miletus is governed by aristocratic men not belonging to any religious cult; religion is kept separate from political life, despite both being public activities. Moreover, although the poems of Homer and Hesiod are a common heritage of all Greek cities, the absence of a single and hereditary caste of priestly class (as happens in Egypt for millennia) does not facilitate the creation of a dogma common to all of Greece, to be followed and from which not to deviate. Each city enjoys its own albeit not total autonomy. To put it with McKirahan1, there is no Bible in the Greek world.

It is in this polis, in this cultural, mercantile, political, religious environment, that the three philosophers of this episode live, in which they expound their own visions of the world, of its birth and its nature.

Thales

Remember Pherecydes, the proto-philosopher from the previous episode? It was said of him that he had secret knowledge learned from the Phoenicians.

Also for Thales it is told2 that he had a possible descent from this people: perhaps not on his mother Cleobulina’s side - which seems to be a Greek name, but his father, Examyes, is said to be from a Phoenician family. More likely that his family was originally from Cadmea, a city of Boeotia (mainland Greece) who moved to Boeotia (a region of Asia Minor). In any case, whether he was actually of Phoenician lineage, or whether this belonging was imposed on him later, the link between Greek science and technology and the Phoenicians does not end with the mythical period.

Thales was born around 624 BCE, in his city he is a man of government and an “engineer”: various stories are told about him, to represent him in often opposite ways; all tell us something about him as a philosopher.

In a dialogue by Plato3 it is reported that a slave from Thrace tells of when Thales ended up in a well, because he was more engaged in observing the sky than in realising what was happening under his feet. Another tale speaks of when he managed to foresee that he would profit from the yield of oil and bought many olive groves of the time at low prices, drawing great profit from it.

Both of these stories speak of practical consequences of Thales’ “philosophical” life: even if probably legendary, the first tells of the “philosopher in the clouds,” far from common life; the second of a man who with his studies and calculations could have drawn enormous riches. On this point, Aristotle explains that Thales was not attracted to it, despite being more than well-off - after all, he was a member of the city aristocracy - since it is not in his interest to enrich himself thanks to his ingenuity.

Regarding his philosophical activity, his travels to Egypt are recounted. This is not hard to believe, since during this period Miletus has some colonies in those areas, including Naucratis4. According to Diogenes Laertius, who cites Eudemus, Thales wrote “ On the Solstice ” and “ On the Equinox ” - texts we do not have. As will also happen with others among the philosophers we will discuss, their theories are known exclusively through secondhand or thirdhand accounts, with all the imprecisions and modifications to the original thought that this entails.

Herodotus reports that Thales is famous for being the first in Greece to predict a solar eclipse, between 585 and 584 BCE, the period in which his acme is placed, that is, the highest point of his philosophical activity. This is debatable, and it is even more so if one imagines that he predicted the exact day5.

Thales is the one who brought abstract geometry to Greece , affirms A. Koestler6. From where? From Egypt and Mesopotamia. Precisely in Mesopotamia it is at least since 721 BCE that solstices and lunations are recorded7, from which one can calculate approximately when an eclipse will occur8; it is not strange for a scholar of that era in Greece to visit the city of Sardis, in Asia Minor, which the Hittites called Uda, thus contacts with Babylonian civilisation are achievable. This is recorded in a similar way also by Theon of Smyrna, who reports Thales’ knowledge of the different duration that the different seasons have during the course of the year.

For Thales, solar eclipses occur when the Sun is covered by the Moon; similarly, when the Earth is between the Moon and the Sun, lunar eclipses occur.

Thales knows when the solstices will occur: this seems feasible, with a suitable instrument, the gnomon, of Babylonian derivation and which Eudemus recounts allowed the philosopher to measure the height of the pyramids, using the principle of proportions between the length of the shadow of an object and that of the pyramid at the same hour of the day, with the actual measurements of that object and the pyramid.

According to Callimachus9, Thales observes Ursa Minor starting from Ursa Major, as the Phoenicians do, and advises Greek sailors to use this method to orient themselves and find north10.

The study of Babylonian tables to predict upcoming eclipses; the measurement of the days that pass between a solstice and the following equinox; the study of the positions of stars in asterisms and constellations: all these things compose the study of ἀστρονομία, astronomy.

Thales goes further, and constructs a cosmological model. We know its (few) details from the works in which it is described (and criticised) by Aristotle: the “De cælo” and the “Metaphysics.” Aristotle attributes to Thales the phrase “all things are full of gods,” McKirahan speaks of a Thales with a Janus face, dual. The Thales of whom Aristotle speaks describes a world in which the things that happen are the work of Nature: the Earth floats on water, and the principle of all things is water.

The first conception is probably of Egyptian and Babylonian derivation. The second is an interpretation by Aristotle, who connects the concept of ἀρχή, the first principle, to water for Thales as an answer to the question: what is the principle of all things? The questions seem to be two in reality, namely: what generates all things ? and where do all things come from ?

The answer therefore for Thales is water, understood not only as the liquid we drink or that flows in a river or that forms the sea, but as a fluid that generates life: it is connected to rain, to semen, to bodily fluids and to everything that flows. Here Thales evokes the Babylonian tradition of water as generating power, and the Egyptian one of creative water. But in Thales there is nothing magical or religious: water is part of the Nature from which everything comes. It is the origin of everything, but it has an origin that is part of this world and that we can observe.

How is the world formed starting from water? Thales does not say, and we do not know his cosmogony, but we know that the Sun and Moon are made of the same substance that composes the Earth, having detached from it after creation. The Earth is a cylindrical disk, perhaps a bit concave - this vision would be consistent with the Babylonian vision of the world according to which the Earth is surrounded by mountains, before being surrounded by Okeanos - which rests on water.

It is stationary in the Universe, and the Sun, Moon and stars perform their motions around it, and when they are no longer visible beyond the horizon they circle around the Earth to return to rise in the east. The light of the Moon is the reflection of the light that arrives from the Sun, and air is formed from the evaporation of water.

Thales shares the vision not only of Babylon and Egypt, but also that of Homer and Hesiod, who tell of a flat Earth that rests on water.

Unlike them, and this is why we define him a philosopher , he wonders what lies beyond , seeks explanations in Nature and not in myths, tries to imagine causes . He does this for example with earthquakes, whose cause he attributes to the movement of the water that supports the Earth. This is a surprising novelty: Thales attempts to explain an observable event by resorting to a non-observable hypothesis , but consistent with his vision of the world. What is missing to complete the requirements of the scientific method? The experiment to be performed to verify the hypothesis - which in any case would have refuted the hypothesis11.

Thales searches for the raw material that composes the world. That he was wrong in identifying it is not important, for the journey we have undertaken.
