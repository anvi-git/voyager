city, transported by the lives of these thinkers, and by the concept common to all of them, that <em>ideas</em> are discussed and <em>do not constitute dogma</em>.</p><p>Arthur Koestler, whom we have fundamental in morality and philosophy.</p><p>We will now speak of the philosophers of Miletus - the Ionians of whom Koestler speaks, the philosophers of Nature, starting with Thales.</p><p>But first, Especially in this way, and with the settlements in Asia Minor, the citizens of the poleis come into contact with other populations, coming from Mesopotamia, or from Egypt, or from Asia Minor itself.</p><p>Miletus is governed by aristocratic men not belonging to any religious cult; religion is kept separate from political life, despite both being public activities. Moreover, although the poems of Homer and Hesiod are a common heritage of all Greek cities, the absence of a single and hereditary caste of priestly class (as happens in Egypt for millennia) does not facilitate the creation of a dogma common to all of Greece, to be followed and from which not to deviate. Each city enjoys its own albeit not total autonomy. To put it with McKirahan<a class="footnote-anchor" data-component-name="FootnoteAnchorToDOM" id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1" target="_self">1</a>, there is no <em>Bible</em> in the Greek world.</p><div><hr></div><p>It is in this polis, in this cultural, mercantile, political, religious environment, that the three philosophers of this episode live, in which they expound their own visions of the world, of its birth and its nature.</p><div><hr></div><h3>Thales</h3><p>Remember Pherecydes, the proto-philosopher from the previous episode? We know its (few) details from the works in which it is described (and criticised) by Aristotle: the &#8220;De c&#230;lo&#8221; and the &#8220;Metaphysics.&#8221; Aristotle attributes to Thales the phrase &#8220;all things are full of gods,&#8221; McKirahan speaks of a Thales with a Janus face, dual. The Thales of whom Aristotle speaks describes a world in which the things that happen are the work of Nature: the Earth floats on water, and the principle of all things is water.</p><p>The first conception is probably of Egyptian and Babylonian derivation. The second is an interpretation by Aristotle, who connects the concept of &#7936;&#961;&#967;&#942;, the first principle, to water for Thales as an answer to the question: what is the principle of all things? Thales does not say, and we do not know his cosmogony, but we know that the Sun and Moon are made of the same substance that composes the Earth, having detached from it after creation. The Earth is a cylindrical disk, perhaps a bit concave - this vision would be consistent with the Babylonian vision of the world according to which the Earth is surrounded by mountains, before being surrounded by Okeanos - which rests on water.</p><p>It is stationary in the Universe, and the Sun, Moon and stars perform their motions around it, and when they are no longer visible beyond the horizon they circle around the Earth to return to rise in the east. He does this for example with earthquakes, whose cause he attributes to the movement of the water that supports the Earth. This is a surprising novelty: Thales attempts to explain an <em>observable</em> <strong>event</strong> by resorting to a <em>non-observable</em> <strong>hypothesis</strong>, but <em>consistent</em> with his vision of the world. What is missing to complete the requirements of the scientific method? The experiment to be performed to verify the hypothesis - which in any case would have refuted the hypothesis<a class="footnote-anchor" data-component-name="FootnoteAnchorToDOM" id="footnote-anchor-11" href="#footnote-11" target="_self">11</a>.</p><p>Thales searches for the raw material that composes the world.
