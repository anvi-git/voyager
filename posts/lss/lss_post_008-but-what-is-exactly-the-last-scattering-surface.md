So &#8220;recombination era&#8221; is just a funny name.</p><p>The components of the Universe at the time of our investigation are primarily photons, protons, electrons, and hydrogen atoms.</p><p>Unlike the early days, a colder Universe is not fully ionized. There are single charges freely flowing in space, and that&#8217;s why the rate of scattering<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-3" href="#footnote-3" target="_self">3</a> falls. </p><p>When the rate of ionization has fallen, the photons have nothing to interact with and don&#8217;t scatter with the matter, starting to &#8220;<strong>free stream</strong><a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-4" href="#footnote-4" target="_self">4</a>&#8221; into the Universe as it expands.</p><figure><img src="https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bcCk!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd6b8c6e7-4e99-4b30-bab2-0b0f738cef47_4396x2548.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>The anisotropies of the Cosmic microwave background (CMB) as observed by the WMAP mission. This means that when recombination started, and finished the era of matter-radiation interaction, the CMB had a different temperature. </p><p>One can calculate the time <strong>t</strong> this happened, the temperature <strong>T</strong> at which this happened, and the relative <strong>redshift</strong><a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-5" href="#footnote-5" target="_self">5</a>.</p><div class="latex-rendered"></div><p>Now, let&#8217;s be said that is not like CMB photons won&#8217;t <strong>ever </strong>interact with <strong>anything anymore. </strong>It&#8217;s just that this won&#8217;t happen as frequently as when the radiation was coupled with matter.</p><p>So this sea of photons will continue to travel through the Universe as it expands, cooling and expanding.</p><p>To visualize this surface, we could first try to look at the best image we have of the background radiation (parametrized by its temperature) as it is today.
