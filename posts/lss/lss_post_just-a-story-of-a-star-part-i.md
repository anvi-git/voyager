Yes, there is a change in the sky season after season, but that may be due to the motion of the Earth around the Sun. </p><p>The Sun looks like a stable, unaltered, never-changing object in the sky. But with the advent of astronomical observations, and therefore with the advent of the telescope, the enhanced observation of the Sun showed that the Sun has spots <em>(sunspots) </em>: the stars were changing. </p><form class="subscription-widget-subscribe"><input type="email" class="email-input" name="email" placeholder="Type your email…" tabindex="-1"><input type="submit" class="button primary" value="Subscribe"><div class="fake-input-wrapper"><div class="fake-input"></div><div class="fake-button"></div></div></form></div></div><p>The history of Solar and especially the sunspot’s observations is very intriguing: it appears for the first time in written text in the <em>I Ching (the Books of Changes), </em>a Chinese divination text written in the late 9th century BCE<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1" target="_self">1</a>, where it’s found</p><blockquote><p>a <em>dou </em>is seen in the Sun</p><p>a <em>mei </em>is seen in the Sun</p></blockquote><p>where <em>dou </em>and <em>mei </em>are darkening or obscuration.</p><p>Also in ancient Greek, around 300 BCE, <strong>Theophrastus </strong>mentioned, first in the West, this type of event. Then other intellectuals (whether they be proper astronomers, monks, jurists, or historians) noted or cited this strange phenomenon. </p><p>The first observation of a sunspot with a telescope was made by <strong>Thomas Harriot</strong>, an English astronomer, on 18 December 1610.  On 9 March 1611, Frisian Johann Goldsmid (latinised Johannes Fabricius) also observed sunspots. </p><p>These two discoveries were shadowed by the claims made by <strong>Galileo Galilei</strong> and <strong>Christoph Scheiner</strong> to have discovered these sunspots in 1612. Whoever discovered first between the two astronomers, they were beaten (without knowing it) by Harriot. </p><h2>Where we came, there we’ll go</h2><p>This brief introduction served to describe the realization that came with the discovery, thanks to the telescope (that rendered unlikely to infer that the sunspots were instead planetary transits), of little changes in the structures, or at least the appearances, of our star the Sun.</p><p>We will now delve into minute details in order to understand how stars are made, how they evolve, how they change, and ultimately how they die. </p><p>We could say that the evolution of stars is somewhat cyclic.</p><p>Stars are formed from Interstellar Medium (ISM) and during their life they expel much of their mass back to the ISM itself, even returning completely to the ISM in the case of Supernovae explosions. </p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank" href="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc4119804-d75a-45a5-a387-854365844c95_1800x1164.jpeg"><div class="image2-inset"><picture><source type="image/webp" srcset="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc4119804-d75a-45a5-a387-854365844c95_1800x1164.jpeg 424w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc4119804-d75a-45a5-a387-854365844c95_1800x1164.jpeg 848w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc4119804-d75a-45a5-a387-854365844c95_1800x1164.jpeg 1272w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc4119804-d75a-45a5-a387-854365844c95_1800x1164.jpeg 1456w" sizes="100vw"><img src="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc4119804-d75a-45a5-a387-854365844c95_1800x1164.jpeg" alt="Image"></picture></a><figcaption class="image-caption"><strong>The Milky Way at 5000 Meters. Credit &amp; Copyright: </strong>Serge Brunier</figcaption></figure></div><p>The dominant component (approximately 70% of the mass) of the ISM is hydrogen gas, in the form of neutral hydrogen (HI), ionized hydrogen (HII), and molecular hydrogen (H2). The other 30% comprises essentially helium; metals<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-2" href="#footnote-2" target="_self">2</a> account for a few percent of the total. </p><p>ISM presents in different ranges of density, masses, and temperatures, mostly because of the different ways for the hydrogen gas. </p><p>In clouds of gas where the hydrogen is primarily atomic, also called <strong>diffuse HI clouds</strong>, with the following properties:</p><ul><li><p>temperatures vary from 30 to 80 K; </p></li><li><p>densities  between 100 and 800 million particles per cubic meter; </p></li><li><p>masses between 1-100 solar masses<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-3" href="#footnote-3" target="_self">3</a>. </p></li></ul><p>When in these clouds one can find molecular hydrogen in the inner regions, these are called <strong>diffuse molecular clouds</strong>, with </p><ul><li><p>temperatures vary from 15 to 50 K;</p></li><li><p>densities between 500 million and 5 billion particles per cubic meter; </p></li><li><p>masses between 3-100 solar masses;</p></li></ul><p>these clouds measure several parsecs<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-4" href="#footnote-4" target="_self">4</a>.</p><p>There are also <strong>Giant molecular clouds, </strong>huge complexes of dust and gas with</p><ul><li><p>temperatures typically of 15 K;</p></li><li><p>densities between 100 and 300 million particles per cubic meter; </p></li><li><p>masses between 100’000 solar masses (up to 1 million solar masses);</p></li></ul><p>these clouds measure 50 parsecs. </p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank" href="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b6c1e95-cbdc-4235-8031-daf1c572d191_3019x2968.jpeg"><div class="image2-inset"><picture><source type="image/webp" srcset="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b6c1e95-cbdc-4235-8031-daf1c572d191_3019x2968.jpeg 424w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b6c1e95-cbdc-4235-8031-daf1c572d191_3019x2968.jpeg 848w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b6c1e95-cbdc-4235-8031-daf1c572d191_3019x2968.jpeg 1272w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b6c1e95-cbdc-4235-8031-daf1c572d191_3019x2968.jpeg 1456w" sizes="100vw"><img src="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b6c1e95-cbdc-4235-8031-daf1c572d191_3019x2968.jpeg" alt="Image"></picture></a><figcaption class="image-caption">HorseHead Nebula, part of the Orion giant molecular cloud. <strong>clumps </strong>(30 solar masses, density of 1 billion particles per cubic meter, temperatures of 10 K, scale of a couple of pc); <strong>dense cores </strong>(10 solar masses, density of 10 billion particles per cubic meter, temperatures of 10 K, scale of a 0.1 pc); Much of the temperature rise comes from <strong>cosmic rays</strong>, mostly charged particles that come from supernova explosions and that heat the ISM in the clouds<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-6" href="#footnote-6" target="_self">6</a>. </p><h2>The formation of Protostars</h2><p>The question we would like to tackle now is the following: </p><p>what are the conditions that lead to the collapse of the globules and cores in order to create the so-called <em>protostars</em>?</p><p>One way to describe this was found by English Astronomer Sir James Jeans, who considered what would happen if a star that doesn’t rotate, not affected by galactic magnetic fields, and without turbulent motion, deviated from hydrostatic equilibrium. The concept used is very simple: if there are two main sources of motion, <strong>gravitational potential energy </strong>and <strong>kinetic energy </strong>of the molecular clouds, these two energies will compete to dominate; if the former dominates, the cloud will collapse, but if the latter prevails, the cloud will expand<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-7" href="#footnote-7" target="_self">7</a>.</p><p>Now all it takes is to understand what is the value of these energies. </p><p>If one assumes the cloud of spherical shape, and of constant density, then the gravitational potential energy is approximable to </p><div class="latex-rendered"></div><p>where <em>G</em> is the gravitational constant, <em>M</em> is the mass of the cloud, and <em>R</em> is its radius.</p><p>The kinetic energy can be estimated to be</p><div class="latex-rendered"></div><p>where <em>N</em> is the total number of particles that compose the cloud, <em>k</em> is the Boltzmann constant, and T is the temperature. The number of particles is derived from the mass of the cloud, divided by the mass of the hydrogen atom and by the <em>mean molecular weight</em> (that is a quantity used to describe what kind of elements compose the cloud).</p><p>When equating these two relations one can find the so-called <strong>Jeans criterion, </strong>both for mass and for size, that decides the maximum mass and the maximum radius the cloud can have in order to not encounter collapse. You can, of course, view them as the minimum mass and radius in order for the cloud to collapse (your choice).</p><p>Let’s report an example from<em> BoB</em><a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-8" href="#footnote-8" target="_self">8</a>: for a cloud with a temperature of 50K, entirely composed of neutral Hydrogen and a number density of 500 million particles per cubic meter, the minimum Jeans mass for collapse is 1500 solar masses. </p><p>But this value is many times the maximum observed mass of the HI clouds: this means that these clouds are stable against gravitational collapse. </p><p>The situation may be somewhat different for a giant molecular cloud, with a temperature of 10K and a number density of ionized hydrogen of 10 billion particles per cubic meter. In this case, the Jeans mass for collapse is 8 solar masses, similar to the order of 10 solar masses for these objects: that’s why GMCs are possible sites of star formation, being unstable against gravitational collapse. </p><h2>What will happen?</h2><p>In the next chapter, we will go on tackling the collapse of unstable molecular clouds and their fragmentation, and follow the first step of the <em>protostar</em>.</p><p>Stay tuned!</p><div><hr></div><div><hr></div><p>Some links for further reading:</p><ul><li><p>https://web.archive.org/web/20060301083022/http://web.hao.ucar.edu/public/education/sp/great_moments.html</p></li><li><p>https://articles.adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-iarticle_query?bibcode=2007JBAA..117..346V&amp;db_key=AST&amp;page_ind=0&amp;data_type=GIF&amp;type=SCREEN_VIEW&amp;classic=YES</p></li><li><p>https://web.archive.org/web/20150102174227/http://www.hao.ucar.edu/education/TimelineB.php</p><p></p></li></ul><div><hr></div><iframe class="spotify-wrap album" src="https://open.spotify.com/embed/album/7o4mIRr1MVq4dMgQiIsUw7" frameborder="0" gesture="media" allowfullscreen="true" allow="encrypted-media" loading="lazy"></iframe><p>See you soon!
