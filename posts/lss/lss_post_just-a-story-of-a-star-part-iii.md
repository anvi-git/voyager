Photons<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-1" href="#footnote-1" target="_self">1</a> can travel great distances before being absorbed by “dust”<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-2" href="#footnote-2" target="_self">2</a>, so we are in an optically thin environment: this means that the collapse is <strong>isothermal</strong>. But the density is not homogeneous, hence there is a slight increase of the free-fall velocity in the center than in the outer parts: this is what we call an <strong>inside-out collapse</strong>). At some point, the density in the center of the cloud becomes high enough<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-3" href="#footnote-3" target="_self">3</a> that the region becomes optically thick<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-4" href="#footnote-4" target="_self">4</a> and the collapse is more adiabatic.</p><p>Now in the inner parts of the cloud, the pressure rises, and the adiabatic collapse <strong>reduces </strong>the rate of the collapse. This region enters hydrostatic equilibrium<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-5" href="#footnote-5" target="_self">5</a> and has a radius of 5 AU<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-6" href="#footnote-6" target="_self">6</a>. </p><p>Did you come this far? Congratulations, you saw the birth of a <strong>protostar</strong>.</p><div><hr></div><p>This object is now observable as a single entity and can be put on maybe the most important plot that a stellar physicist encounters in their life: that is, the Hertzsprung-Russel diagram. </p><p>What is it? What does it tell us? Why it is important? </p><p>All these questions (that I’m sure you were asking yourselves) will be answered swiftly. </p><div><hr></div><h2>Digression: Hertzsprung-Russell diagram</h2><h4>or, how to draw points and curves on a plot</h4><p>The idea of putting stars on a plot arrived in the early twentieth century when astronomers noticed that the observed stars varied in luminosities and absolute magnitudes. This led to the idea that stars begin their “life” with a certain amount of “<em>fuel”</em>, that is, material that can be transformed in luminosity and energy, and as they age they cool off, becoming colder and colder.</p><p>This idea is nowadays considered wrong; nonetheless, it helped astronomy progress.</p><p>In 1905 Danish amateur astronomer Ejnar Hertzsprung (1873-1967) confirmed that there was indeed a correlation between the absolute magnitudes<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-7" href="#footnote-7" target="_self">7</a> and the spectral type<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-8" href="#footnote-8" target="_self">8</a> of a sample of stars. He noticed also that stars with the same spectral type had different magnitudes, i.e. had different levels of brightness.</p><p>He named the brighter stars <strong>giants</strong> because if you consider stars as great converters of energy in luminosity, then if two stars have the same temperature, and the most luminous must also be the bigger one<a class="footnote-anchor" id="footnote-anchor-9" href="#footnote-9" target="_self">9</a>.</p><p>This relation was still not plotted on a graph.
