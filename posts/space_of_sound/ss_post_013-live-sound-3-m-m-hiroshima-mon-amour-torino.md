I went to Iceland a few months ago, and the experience was both transcending and deeply rooted in nature. I’m constantly thinking back to those moments, and frequently picturing the places I’ve been, with the soundtrack of the music we listened to while driving - many of those songs are in this newsletter, by the way.

I’m not the only one to think that the performance by múm , Icelandic folk-rock band yesterday in concert in Turin, was the bridge that allowed us to go back to those moments not just picturing images in our minds, but also listening to otherworldly sounds.
![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1r7v!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/46ed9569-eb4f-484d-98cd-525cd1761b20_3024x4032.heic)
The opening act was performed by Bjorn Daniel, an Iceland artist now living in Turin. A raw, intimate performance, and a passionate one, because Daniel was opening for one of his adolescence’s dream bands. Hiroshima Mon Amour, Torino (November 12, 2025).

Music can be encoded and decoded, like a message. And it is a message, which contains information about the sender, and - hopefully - should also contain some message for the receiver.
múm perform in a way that openly labels them as something different from bands from other places: like Sigur Rós, to say of another Icelandic band, you hear there is a different background.
múm sing and play like a delicate ensemble, only to explode in a shoegaze-like conundrum of sounds: one moment the stillness of a glacier, the next lava roars and a mountain erupts.
The songs are long exposure photos, they stay on the retina of your ears, they impress on your listenings.
They are both lullabies and marches, both quiet and explosive.
Fire and ice, like the Iceland they come from.
