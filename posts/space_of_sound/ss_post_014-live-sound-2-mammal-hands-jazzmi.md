Getting to know a band one day and attending their concert eight days later: that’s what happened to me with Mammal Hands , a jazz group based in London and formed in Norwich in 2012, with five albums with Gondwana Records.

In the last week I had listened to all the songs, created a playlist with all the tracks from the first album to the last, and had my favorite.
From the moment they stepped on stage, the atmosphere became mystical and it felt like being in a time outside of time, the atmosphere that the three musicians created built a spiral of sounds that carried you away and drew you in. Both at the same time, music is a special thing.
![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Q4GN!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/10b83648-c47d-43e5-b00a-9c6b753c29fe_3024x4032.heic)
Mammal Hands on stage @ JAZZMI, Nov 5, 2025 (Triennale Milano, Milan)
It felt like breathing with them, keeping time, jumping hunched over the piano.
I don’t know much about jazz - actually, I don’t know much about music in the technical sense, I talk about it insofar as it seems to convey something to me and when that happens I like to share it - but from what I saw, from what I heard!, there’s a formidable technique.
The songs are complex structures, rhythmic, coherent and fractal: a marvel for the ears.

At the end of the concert, lasting just under an hour and a half, I bought a vinyl with my favorite song by the group, and with the three - Nick Smart , Jordan Smart , and Rob Turner - I chatted a bit, just long enough to get the cover signed, and compliment them on the spectacular concert.
