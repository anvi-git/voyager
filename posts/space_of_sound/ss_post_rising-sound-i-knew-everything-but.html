October will bring you fresh records, new song reviews - you’ll see very soon, starting from today.

Zac Vanacoro is a music artist, singer, bass player and member of rock bands based in New York and halfway the world: Velvet Weekend, Plane Station, Austin Kopec, Christine Chanel and Smooch.

Today his new solo album comes out: I Knew Everything But I Forgot It .

The surprise I had when I received my first press kit mail , presenting the artist and its work, with a link to listen to the tracks before the publication, was a wonderful feeling - and I thank you readers for that, too.

That mail came through the Internet, crossing half the world1, and I got to work immediately. Meaning, I listened to the songs over and over.

This is what I think of it.

here the playlist on Apple Music.

There and Back Again is a morning call, an alarm clock ringing alone: we know where we’re going, but “something doesn’t feel quite right”. And where has the time gone, that keeps running away? The questions and doubts dissolve into a final chorus, which like a circle returns to where it came from, there and back again, precisely.

In Troubled Mind someone heals our mind full of problems, and everything falls back into place: the chorus keeps repeating it, and the finger-picking in the second part seems to paint a smile between two cheeks. Everything’s alright, then.

With Chianti and Fava Beans it feels like turning on a radio to a different decade, having just turned onto a noir street, and ending up in a jazz club. The notes played and immediately withdrawn, the chorus sung in company, and the general spirit of the song, together with the horns in the second part, accompany the end of an afternoon while we prepare a party. With a bottle of Chianti, of course.

Everything Stays is more delicate, slower, we’re full of thoughts as we listen to it: all our experiences stay in their place, the lyrics suggest. The piano notes accompanying the second verse are a brief window opened, but immediately closed again, onto the river of our thoughts. If it were a house, this song would have two windows open facing each other, and the wind would pass between them. Is it a current, if it doesn’t pass through you? Really, it passes through the final chorus, with full and moving strings.

April wasn’t born in April, but feels that’s their home, wants to return there. But it talks about what happens in the meantime, between November and April again, and the journey is metaphorical, but we feel spring in the choruses and guitars.

Coffee!

Over and Over begins with a guitar, continues with a guitar, two voices join in, we’re in a room, or we’re around a bonfire, then a piano is added, and then we’re in front of a fireplace, because we still feel that warmth. The guitar strumming wraps around us, over and over again.

Daisy starts straight, in the evening, a drum kit, the usual full guitar and again a voice that cascades from one verse to another: but it’s not the usual song, an electric guitar is introduced, and the distortions reconstruct it. A live song, it would seem.

Amen talks about having lost your head in the sand, on the shoreline. And having lost your head, in a metaphorical sense, up in the sky. And then the prayer to find a god in wine, and the song becomes almost an electric-gospel-for-Friday-night. I’d go to that bar, if I knew which one it was.

Only Losers Left Alive is advice, an open letter, you have to be careful: you only fall when you’ve climbed somewhere. Its eight minutes are a journey, a procession, a band in celebration, a fair, a festival. They’re a soundtrack and end credits, the ending and the whole journey. In this journey, it’s normal to feel “ tired “. The song never tires though, with the second part being an incredible ride, worth the entire album. Truly remarkable.

When I Get To Heaven is a blues vocal, in brevity and style.

Lady In The Radiator reassures us, everything’s fine in heaven. And everything seems to suggest it, but with a slightly eerie twist: the processed voice, the suspended rhythm, overflowing and somewhat nocturnal drum beat recalls a ghost you might have encountered at night at the end of October. It fully belongs to the songs you might have heard coming-from-the-bathroom-of-a-nightclub.

My Oh My talks about growing up “Oh, fuck!”. The older you get the fewer people you know. It seems like a singer’s song in a sing-along at the end of a concert. Maybe it’s too delicate in the final part for that kind of thing, but I wouldn’t mind.

I got surprised when I got that mail, as I said before. The surprise grew bigger while listening to the record, gaining a fresh angle with respect to my usual listenings. Not that this is completely an alien sound, but it gave me new perspectives.

The idea I got from the whole record is one of dedication and love to play in order to experiment , and this is certainly a plus. Music, as all art, must replicate the world, which is ever-changing.

This record does so sincerely and open-heartedly, and I hope to listen to Zac’s string live soon to change one more time.
