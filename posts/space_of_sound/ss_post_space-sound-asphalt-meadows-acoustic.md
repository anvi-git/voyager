<blockquote><p><em><strong>At the gate, you handed me<br>Pieces of the plan</strong></em></p></blockquote><p>Bellingham, Washington. 1997. <strong>Ben Gibbard</strong> has so many things to say, and he starts a solo project. The name <strong><a href="https://www.deathcabforcutie.com/">Death Cab For Cutie</a></strong> comes from the title of a song performed in 1967 by <strong>Bonzo Dog Doo-Dah Band.</strong></p><p>In 2002, <strong>Chris Walla</strong> joined on guitar, <strong>Nathan Good</strong> on drums, and <strong>Nick Harmer</strong> on bass guitar. <strong>Jayson Tolzdorf-Larson</strong> and <strong>Michael Schorr</strong> appeared and left around 2000, mostly on tour. <strong>Jason McGerr</strong> joined the band in 2003, and <strong>Zac Rae</strong> in 2015 on keyboards and guitar, along with <strong>Dave Depper.</strong></p><div><hr></div><p><strong><a href="https://open.spotify.com/album/6hRYTGSMlOgLU4KxcHFMJK?si=SixOkvroRU2FHf8YLYTi-A">Asphalt Meadows</a></strong> is the band’s tenth album, and I’m writing about the acoustic version, released in 2023, a year after the original version.</p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank" href="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40d367a6-3bf8-4ea9-a322-6be67c5fbf17_1400x1400.jpeg"><div class="image2-inset"><picture><source type="image/webp" srcset="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40d367a6-3bf8-4ea9-a322-6be67c5fbf17_1400x1400.jpeg 424w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40d367a6-3bf8-4ea9-a322-6be67c5fbf17_1400x1400.jpeg 848w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40d367a6-3bf8-4ea9-a322-6be67c5fbf17_1400x1400.jpeg 1272w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40d367a6-3bf8-4ea9-a322-6be67c5fbf17_1400x1400.jpeg 1456w" sizes="100vw"><img src="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40d367a6-3bf8-4ea9-a322-6be67c5fbf17_1400x1400.jpeg" alt="Image"></picture></a><figcaption class="image-caption">Asphalt Meadows (acoustic) - Death Cab For Cutie (2023, Atlantic)</figcaption></figure></div><p>You should<em> “listen to the ringing in your ears / the scrambled voices of your fears / whispering, whispering”</em>, Gibbard sings in the song that opens the record, <em>I Don’t Know How I Survive</em>. He doesn’t know how to survive, and the song is a gentle introduction to that refrain. The song is “<em>gaining speed</em>”, and while it does, you start listening “<em>to the sound of your heartbeat”</em>. </p><p>Gibbard knows you’ve been struggling, and <em>Roman Candles</em> is a follow-up to the night before, to the song before. </p><p>It’s one of those mornings where it’s “<em>a battle just to wake and greet the day”</em>, but you have to<em> “learn to let go”</em>, not to let all the things you “<em>tried to hold”</em> “<em>explode like roman candles</em>”. And maybe he should use some “<em>flames”</em>, because now he can’t “<em>feel anything”</em>, he’s like a rock in a river, “<em>standing still”</em> while everyone else is moving.</p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank" href="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9f4ce96-f247-4165-a504-15ddc46abb68_1581x1054.webp"><div class="image2-inset"><picture><source type="image/webp" srcset="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9f4ce96-f247-4165-a504-15ddc46abb68_1581x1054.webp 424w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9f4ce96-f247-4165-a504-15ddc46abb68_1581x1054.webp 848w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9f4ce96-f247-4165-a504-15ddc46abb68_1581x1054.webp 1272w, https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9f4ce96-f247-4165-a504-15ddc46abb68_1581x1054.webp 1456w" sizes="100vw"><img src="https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9f4ce96-f247-4165-a504-15ddc46abb68_1581x1054.webp" alt="Image"></picture></a></figure></div><p>He may need someone else, someone from whom to get a “<em>kiss”</em> as “<em>a lonely prayer”</em>. He would cherish that kiss and hold it in his eyes and his mind, although you can “<em>only know half of”</em> that person. <em>Asphalt Meadows</em> does exactly that, and it’s a beautifully written song; it should be written on bathroom walls to let someone make good decisions. The chorus is worth the entire record, and you should do yourself a favor and listen to it. </p><p>And people are like planes, always “<em>drifting off into the sky”</em>: and what if you have a “<em>ticket”</em> to board, but you lack the name of the plane you should take? </p><p>You should ride a wave of “<em>white noise”</em> when the city is sleeping, and find light through the concrete of those buildings, or board a train instead, before the doors close. Beware not to get<em> “trampled under your own feet”</em>.</p><p><em>Rand McNally</em> is an American technology company, and the name of the next song. An atlas of such a company is something Gibbard does not “<em>know how to use”; </em>but he made a sweet song about it, so I have to correct him. In a sense, he carves meaning out of such an ordinary book like it were the “<em>Old Testament”</em>. And this song has verses, too. </p><p><em>“Here to Forever”</em> is not only a beautiful promise, it’s also what Gibbard sings to want to “<em>know the measure”</em> of: it’s a daring desire, something that would put a lot of pressure on you, be it “<em>God or whatever”</em>.</p><p><em>Foxglove Through The Clearcut </em>is a declaration about the settlers, the frontier man who explores until the edge of America, only to realize there is “<em>nowhere left to go”</em>, and that everything has been “<em>broken”</em> and that this “<em>great land”</em> “<em>slowly slip[</em>s<em>] away”.</em></p><p>Is it a funeral without the flowers? Are clear-cut trees in a forest like “<em>tombstones”</em>? At least, they are “<em>reaching for the sky”</em>.</p><p><em>Pepper</em> is a song with a faster pace and has a great rhythm. You should end a relationship on this song, while you decide on one another</p><blockquote><p><em><strong>Kiss me just this one last time<br>Tell me that you oncе were mine</strong></em></p></blockquote><p>and one kiss after another, you may end up being together until forever.</p><p><em>I Miss Strangers</em> is a song about the punk war (“<em>you were by my side on the frontlines / do you remember?”</em>), and on the realization that sometimes you can “<em>miss strangers”</em> more than you “<em>miss</em> [your] <em>friends”</em>. </p><p><em>Wheat Like Waves </em>is a song that<em> “I could not pretend to comprehend </em>[…]<em> as a childless man”, </em>but the violin and a gentle drum follow Gibbard’s voice into a lulling song you could play on a road trip where you want to stop being alone.</p><p><em>Fragments From the Decade</em> is for those who “<em>in photos […] are were always staring through the lens / To some distant place […] would rather have been”. </em>And beware: “<em>if you stare too long they almost seem alive</em>”.</p><p><em>I’ll Never Give Up On You </em>is an ode to leaving out of your life all the fake aspirations you can have, to remain with one another. <em>The Plan</em> is a gift someone gives to Ben Gibbard before he boards a plane, and he gives it back to us, in a beautiful little song that closes the record. </p><blockquote><p><em><strong>Can I hold it<br>For a week?</strong></em></p></blockquote><iframe class="spotify-wrap album" src="https://open.spotify.com/embed/album/6hRYTGSMlOgLU4KxcHFMJK" frameborder="0" gesture="media" allowfullscreen="true" allow="encrypted-media" loading="lazy"></iframe><div id="youtube2-fHlbxURn4Uc" class="youtube-wrap"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/fHlbxURn4Uc?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay;
