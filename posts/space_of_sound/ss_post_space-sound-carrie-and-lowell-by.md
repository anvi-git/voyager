Stevens' delicate voice, the gentle piano touch make this song almost a confession (which perhaps it is, after all, because it ends invoking Jesus, to be "near me").</p><div><hr></div><h4>Fuck me, I’m falling apart</h4><div><hr></div><p><em>No Shade in the Shadows of the Cross</em> is the account of how Stevens lived with his mother's passing, of the things he did and those he felt like doing, all mediated by the religious sentiment that drives and penetrates him.</p><div id="youtube2-Xr90QaiX-_U" class="youtube-wrap" data-attrs="{&quot;videoId&quot;:&quot;Xr90QaiX-_U&quot;,&quot;startTime&quot;:null,&quot;endTime&quot;:null}" data-component-name="Youtube2ToDOM"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/Xr90QaiX-_U?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><p>He speaks of the way he felt he had to self-destruct in order to feel close to her, to emulate her self-destructive behaviour in life; he speaks of the brief moments of awareness after episodes of self-harm, substance and alcohol abuse. It's a heartbreaking lament, funereal in melody and rhythm, that delivers the most human and wounded version of Stevens.</p><div><hr></div><h4>Lord, touch me with lightning</h4><div><hr></div><p><em>Blue Bucket of Gold</em> is the album's closing song (not in the 10th anniversary version, which also contains demos and new versions of old tracks). It's a song in which Stevens searches for his mother like one searches for a treasure (the blue bucket of gold is a treasure rumored to be present in Oregon).</p><div id="youtube2-BjJWy_i8hJ8" class="youtube-wrap" data-attrs="{&quot;videoId&quot;:&quot;BjJWy_i8hJ8&quot;,&quot;startTime&quot;:null,&quot;endTime&quot;:null}" data-component-name="Youtube2ToDOM"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/BjJWy_i8hJ8?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><p>He would like to be part of someone's life, he would like something to admire, something to love, he desires a conversion: he asks the Lord to strike him like lightning, like Paul of the Scriptures.</p><p>The song, and side A of the record, close with angelic vocalisations, but at the same time lugubrious, divine but heartbreaking.</p><div><hr></div><p><em>Death With Dignity (Demo)</em>, <em>Should Have Known Better (Demo)</em>, <em>The Only Thing (Demo)</em>, <em>Eugene (Demo)</em> are less structured versions than those present on side A, but Stevens' voice strikes even more; the last one features the collaboration of S. Carey, among other things a member of Bon Iver.</p><div><hr></div><h4>Oh, will wonders ever cease?</h4><div><hr></div><p><em>Mystery of Love (Demo)</em>, a song composed by Stevens for <strong>Luca Guadagnino</strong>'s movie <strong>Call Me By Your Name</strong>, is published here in a version similar to the previous demos: voice and guitar. It speaks of Hephaestion and Alexander the Great separated by death, of not being able to find anyone else. It's a not particularly happy love song, of that love that dare not speak its name.</p><div id="youtube2-DvYSFQyw8q8" class="youtube-wrap" data-attrs="{&quot;videoId&quot;:&quot;DvYSFQyw8q8&quot;,&quot;startTime&quot;:null,&quot;endTime&quot;:null}" data-component-name="Youtube2ToDOM"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/DvYSFQyw8q8?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><div><hr></div><h4>But have you heard the story of my mother’s fate?</h4><div><hr></div><p><em>Wallowa Lake Monster (Version 2)</em> is the account of the way Stevens imagines (or remembers) taking care of a mother, like a heroic undertaking, while another battle was being fought: that of the mother against a monster (abuse?) which in the end, this dragon, got the better of her. "<em>When the dragon submerged we knew she had died</em>". The instrumental outro is incredible.</p><div id="youtube2-_w3cMp1Csx8" class="youtube-wrap" data-attrs="{&quot;videoId&quot;:&quot;_w3cMp1Csx8&quot;,&quot;startTime&quot;:null,&quot;endTime&quot;:null}" data-component-name="Youtube2ToDOM"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/_w3cMp1Csx8?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><div><hr></div><p><em>Fourth of July (Version 4)</em> closes the record.
