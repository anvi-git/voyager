This Cassius is more evidently a reference to Gaius Cassius Longinus, a Roman senator who plotted to kill Caesar, and Muhammad Ali, born in Kinshasa. </p><h6><code>[...I don't think he got it]</code></h6><div id="youtube2-DiAkwKqZ1_E" class="youtube-wrap" data-attrs="{&quot;videoId&quot;:&quot;DiAkwKqZ1_E&quot;,&quot;startTime&quot;:null,&quot;endTime&quot;:null}" data-component-name="Youtube2ToDOM"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/DiAkwKqZ1_E?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><p>Pecknold misses “<em>the highway</em>”, and now I do too, only to drive to Memphis.</p><p>The song ends on a drone sequence, and finally we arrive at the end, on the song which gives the name to the record. </p><h6><code>[Iceberg]</code></h6><h6><code>[Iceberg]</code></h6><p><em>Crack-Up</em> is a song for which the words “<em>won’t come”</em>, but then Pecknold quotes Cicero, and then “all things change”, because the song hits a refrain which leads directly to the outro, where the sea comes back, “<em>rising over me</em>”.</p><h6><br><code>[Exit]</code></h6><div><hr></div><p>This record is a profound experience, but it certainly is not an easy one. </p><p>Pecknold’s lyrics are cryptic and layered in significance, the sound is rarefied and diverse, and the song structure is not plain. It seems like a diary, a conversation, a confession, all in once. </p><p>At the end on the last song there is a recording of someone taking the stairs, or maybe just walking rapidly: where are they going? who are they?</p><p>Guess we’ll never know, but probably they’re going out in the sun, “<em>facing the day”</em>.</p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2
