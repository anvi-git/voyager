<p><strong><a href="https://open.spotify.com/artist/3x8FbPjh2Qz55XMdE2Yalj?si=Bs3EWjwLTvy3D9YWPsa9ow">Nick Mulvey</a></strong> is an artist from Cambridge, England. He founded the band <strong>Portico Quartet</strong>, then left in 2011. </p><p>As a solo composer and musician, he published several albums and singles. </p><p><em><a href="https://open.spotify.com/album/7J8GMfGTfVzoSdZsUgt0Bg?si=p9H4diynS5a9TyNmsyPXJg">Dancing For The Answers</a></em> was released in 2018, under <em>Fiction Records Label</em>.</p><iframe class="spotify-wrap album" src="https://open.spotify.com/embed/album/7J8GMfGTfVzoSdZsUgt0Bg" frameborder="0" gesture="media" allowfullscreen="true" allow="encrypted-media"></iframe><p>I would regard his style as chamber-folk, with a hint of electronica and ethereal arrangements, but I’m open to discussion.</p><div><hr></div><p>The first song is eponym to the record name, and in over eight minutes many things happen: the song gently starts with a guitar-picking with the purpose of introducing the “<em>wonder</em>” which “<em>comes along</em>” “<em>every</em>” new “<em>day</em>”; with Mulvey’s voice also arrives a line of drums. </p><p>The lyrics are almost spoken, and they feel like a prophecy</p><div class="pullquote"><p><em>you'll do all the things for which you came<br>and there's no such thing as time<br>nothing is lost and no one is saved</em></p></div><p> and there’s a sense of discovery, it reads like a poem. </p><div class="pullquote"><p>when you find greatness in the peace that you do not claim<br>you will have it all</p></div><p>When the lyrics end, there is a sense of suspension, almost like when you’re waiting for rain to come in the summer, and then the electronic nature of the song is unveiled, and the song erupts in a techno-ish ending.</p><div id="youtube2-jwxrGGZ9R00" class="youtube-wrap"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/jwxrGGZ9R00?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><div><hr></div><p><em>Give It To Kali</em> comes next, and here guitar riffs arrive since the start. The song isa bit shorter that the previous, but longer then the next (a pattern repeated throughout the record) and it takes its time to build. </p><p>Also in this song, an important part of the lyrics are spoken rather than sung, and the start is a sample devoted to introduce “<em>all the things</em>” that you should offer to Kali, which is the Hindu Goddess of Time, Creation, Power and Destruction. </p><div id="youtube2-kk_ezsy54kY" class="youtube-wrap"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/kk_ezsy54kY?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><p>And it’s a prayer to give her, “<em>really giving it to her</em>”, not faking it and taking it all back. You must be completely devoted (do you remember, “t<em>here's no such thing as time”?).</em></p><p>The spoken song is sustained by percussions and bass lines that bring you to another place, a different place from where you started. A magical thing for a song to do.</p><div><hr></div><p><em>The Doing Is Done</em> is a song on “undoings”: parents shaped by their sons, darkness defined by the day - if you ask me, darkness is also defined by the absence of light, but the sense goes the same. </p><p>But it’s also a song about purpose, and the question concerning that purpose: “<em>where could I go?</em>”, what is left for a mother or a father? </p><div id="youtube2-7t5n9bTPHJc" class="youtube-wrap"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/7t5n9bTPHJc?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><p>What comes next is to live “<em>full cost</em>”, embrace the <em>woods</em> as the place in nature to come back to, or the <em>darkness</em>, that embraces the Earth and the whole Universe. “<em>Pouring honesty”</em> every day.</p><div><hr></div><p><em>Punta Cometa (Dancing For The Answers Alt Version)</em> is an alternative version of the first song, and a line that closes on itself. </p><div id="youtube2-uOynzc10UD4" class="youtube-wrap"><div class="youtube-inner"><iframe src="https://www.youtube-nocookie.com/embed/uOynzc10UD4?rel=0&amp;autoplay=0&amp;showinfo=0&amp;enablejsapi=0" frameborder="0" loading="lazy" gesture="media" allow="autoplay; fullscreen" allowautoplay="true" allowfullscreen="true" width="728" height="409"></iframe></div></div><p>It’s a shorter version, and a more strained one: it looks like the whole journey Mulvey has anticipated in this short EP he eventually has crossed it to reach the end, only to be surprised by the same cycle of birth, life, death and destruction which comes with the goddess Kali and with a cyclical idea of time. </p><p>A rather sophisticated notion, or maybe a narrative expedient and nothing more. Still, the song serves as a reminder, a suggestion or a proposition to endure, go on and continue one’s life.</p><p>If you put this record in loop, you’ll start wandering too.</p><div
