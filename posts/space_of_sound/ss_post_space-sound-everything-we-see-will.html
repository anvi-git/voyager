At The End of Times, Nothing is not just a cosmologically accurate prospect, it's also the title of an instrumental project by Sven Karlsson , a Swedish musician and producer.

Everything We See Will Die is the second EP produced under this name, and it was published in 2016 with the label Epidemic Sound ; the songs are indeed instrumental, composed with electric guitars, drum beats and impetuous crescendos.

The Ground After a Summer Rain has a title with a cyclical meaning connected to the last track (we'll discuss this at the end); after a timid entrance of the drums accompanying the electric guitar, the two chase each other, growing more and more until they reach the point of explosion, doing so and leaving the same instruments on the road, tired, destroyed, tested by the effort.

The Sky is No Limit starts slowly, and here the drums support the rhythm of the guitar, until the opposite happens, and it's the beat that remains confined by the small guitar riffs.

Last Sunrise in the Wasteland is in anxious anticipation of fulfilling itself from the moment the drums enter the scene: and when it does explode with a roar that leaves few seconds before moving on to the next, Once Every 20 Years , which is perhaps the most classic of all, almost restoring a sense of normality that ultimately, once every twenty years, is acceptable. Then a chorus comes to dampen this statement, and the electric guitars rise up and so no, it's not a normal song. A nice discovery.

Sven Karlsson, creator of the project At The End of Times, Nothing

Stuck in the Apocalyptic Vision is the most "hard" song of the album, with a metal sound that suits the title well.

Petrichor is the last song of the album, and completes a circle creating a loop of meanings with the first song, since petrichor is the scent that earth has after an intense rain - a summer one.

The sound becomes a bit more lulling than usual - certainly more than the previous track - and it's truly the right song to listen to in the rain. Which when it ends, will have the scent of Petrichor, and its sound too.
