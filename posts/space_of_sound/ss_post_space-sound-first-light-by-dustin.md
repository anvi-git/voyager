Tebbutt</a></strong> is a musician from Armidale, New South Wales (Australia). </p><p><a href="https://open.spotify.com/album/6IaqqnBr3CzvM0GHtBWQNv?si=8DxyyPRjSbayDOeMvNMfiA">First Light</a> came out name of the first song on the record, and Tebbutt here talks about being in love “<em>as far as one can fall”, </em>evidently hoping not to fall. It’s also a song about waking up and witnessing two sunrises: one outside the window, the other in someone’s eyes. </p><p><em>Wooden Heart</em> is a solid song from the beginning, with the direct line of drums. And it’s direct in addressing that there must be physical signs of your love: a name carved on a tree, for example.</p><p><em>Give Me Tonight</em> is what made me draw a comparison with some works from James Blake. It definitely is the most electronic influenced song in the record, and speaks of the “<em>sacred”</em> moments in which you tell each other your names on a bed: it’s a sensual song about not making love, but in celebrating it. Works as well.</p><p><em>Wild Blood</em> is an invitation to be together, a song to plead “<em>that you should stay this time</em>”. Tebbutt evidently speaks about love in his songs, and this is the main thread: here he speaks about wild blood that maybe is the reason his loved one is running away, and it’s the same thing that draws him to them. </p><p>Because they’re “<em>Still in My Heart</em>”, as he says in the next song. His love is now an “<em>open road</em>”, where the “<em>colours start to run”</em>.</p><p>“<em>See Your Gold</em>” opens the second half of the record with a delicate track of piano, and then a plain guitar-picking. It seems an encore of Wild Blood in the lyrics, because here Tebbutt assures his lover that “<em>I told you, you are enough</em>”, because he sees “<em>gold through the shadows</em>”. It’s a good song to drift away to sleep, as I sometimes recall saying in this newsletter.</p><p>“<em>Hawlett’s Comet</em>” is not Halley’s, but indeed it comes back once in a while to remember you that “<em>This is our love</em>”, assuring Tebbutt that “<em>the charts and stars were right</em>”.</p><p>“<em>In Too Deep”</em> is the next song, and also the feeling we all have when listening to another love song. But love is not a finite thing for Tebbutt, as it seems, and he’s “<em>drift</em>[ing] <em>away from the surface</em>” of this love, only to come back and state, in the next song, that his love is “<em>Brighter Than The Sun</em>”, which is also the title of the next song. Here Tebbutt speaks about his lover as a lake, as a coast, as the entire nature Australia has, that draws him into his love, enduring and accreting it as a forming star. </p><p><em>I Only Have Good Memories (Of You) </em>closes the record, and from the title it could appear as a sad song (would be the first on the record). And indeed it is an instrumental track, but it’s not necessarily a sad song: it has something that seems unresting, in seamless motion, though.
