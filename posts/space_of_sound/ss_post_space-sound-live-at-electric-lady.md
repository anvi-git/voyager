Now you’re just the stranger that I know best
Bleachers is a rock band from New Jersey, formed in 2013 by musician Jack Antonoff . Other members of the band include Mikey Freedom Hart , Evan Smith , Michael Riddleberger , Sean Hutchinson , and Zem Audu.
Live At Electric Lady is from 2021, published with RCA Records . Electric Lady Studios is a recording studio in Greenwich Village, in New York.
![](https://substack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com/public/images/e2015f96-965c-47be-b9c5-6e6d913e8f9f_1000x1000.jpeg)
The record starts with Drive , written by Ric Ocasek. The song is a one-hit classic (it’s not the only hit on this record, if you ask me) where Antonoff lists a series of questions to his partner, the kind of questions we all should ask ourselves, because they deal with the faith we put in each other, to be able to fall, to err, to do stuff wrong, knowing we have someone on our side, that “ pay [s] attention to [our] dreams ”.
Doesn’t matter that the same person we can count on to drive us home, also “ plug [s] their ears up when you scream ”: we’d do the same.
Next comes 45 , like the vinyl format record, when Antonoff in another splendid performance takes us to a stranger’s garden, with blessings and flowers all on their side, having lost (Antonoff) all those provided by that someone he’s referring to in the song.
But like a 45 vinyl that keeps spinning after the end of the record, he’s “ still on your side ”, Antonoff sings.
And analogies with the music are not all in the vinyl format: Antonoff talks about “ praying at the ‘90s' “ while everybody else moves on. All that remains is a shadow, both of that East Coast music , and of a love now lost.
Don’t Go Dark is a song written by Antonoff and Lana del Rey, and is about keeping on during difficulties, when all you want to do is have a good cry; but it’s also a song that it’s direct in advising that help is not coming, at least not from Antonoff (“ If you're holdin' on to me, you're holdin' on to nothing ”). All you get from him is an advice to “ run like the wild” .
Chinatown is a song written by Antonoff and Evan Smith, and performed with Bruce “the Boss” Springsteen , and it’s a love ballad with a great rhythm and solid lines (“ And I’ll wear your sadness like it’s mine ”) where in Chinatown there is a Chinatown girl, that apparently “ rip [s] [Antonoff’s] head off ”, and with whom he wants to run away, to find “ tomorrow” together, “ out of the city” , “ right into the shadow” .
How dare you want more comes next, and it’s written by Antonoff with Patrik Berger (who also co-wrote track #7). The song is a sustained rock-infused and jazz-y influenced parenthesis with nice brass instruments and a genuinely felt atmosphere of companionship and love-in-making-music - notice the ( Alright! I like that take ) ( That was a good one ).
Well, like Antonoff says, “ how dare you want more? ”
Big Life is the one Antonoff is after, like he sings since the first chorus. It’s a song on cars, bikes, on wheels that feel free, on big lights as things to become, as ideas to indulge. It’s also a song on the desire to live a big life with someone and find their secrets, those parts “ that light doesn’t touch” .
What’d I Do With All This Faith? features St. Vincent , and it’s a delicate ballad - plumbous for sure - on a love that can’t be, because no one ever met while one was searching on a “ crowded street” and the other hadn’t “ left [their] room that night ”.
Strange Behavior closes the record, and it’s the perfect closing for a live record that could be played at sunset, and thus could end at night. The strange behavior is of a kind where you think that a girl, a boy, or anyone could save you, and you follow them “ around the world ”. That faith you put in that quest could be just “ hollow space ”, and you’d end up “ chasing shadows ”.
In any case, good luck.
