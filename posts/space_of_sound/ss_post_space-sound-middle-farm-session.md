<p>Sad music is so good to listen to. </p><p>I think many can relate, and it is particularly true when it is produced by someone that you can imagine are listening to their own sad music.</p><div><hr></div><p><strong><a href="https://ohdaughter.com/">Daughter</a></strong> falls in this category: the trio formed by <strong>Elena Tonra</strong>, <strong>Igor Haefeli</strong>,  and <strong>Remi Aguilella </strong>is always on the verge of cracking you up and making you cry, and does so by warmingly encouraging you to accept that not everything must be happy to be beautiful.</p><p>They formed in 2010 and recently released an EP registered at the Middle Farm Studios in Dartmoor, <strong><a href="https://youtu.be/qkbbI13GXgU">Middle Farm Session</a></strong>.</p><div class="captioned-image-container"><figure><a class="image-link image2 is-viewable-img" target="_blank" with <em>Future Lover</em>, where we get caught</p><div class="pullquote"><blockquote><p>listening for sweet nohings from ghost in the room </p></blockquote></div><p>and Tonra presses us to answer her questions, “<em>what’s the future like?”</em>, to which someone replies “<em>wait another year”, </em>and we’re just happy to be interrogated by her voice.</p><p><em>Party </em>is not a song you could party with, but could be the perfect score for an end-of-relationship-kind-of-conversation, making that night “<em>the worst night of </em>[your]<em> life”.</em></p><p><em>To Rage</em> is the perfect sequel to Party, where we’re left tracking the pieces left by those exhausting conversations that explode, when someone storms out of the room and you end up “<em>distraught”</em>.</p><p><em>Be on your way</em> is the perfect therapy session you need after that, when you make sense of what happened, of the planets that mark the distance between you two; and as long as you try to “<em>be on your way”</em>, you’ll find it difficult to “<em>reconstruct the scene</em>”. </p><p>Dandelion exits from that loop, and the music of Haefeli and Aguilella follows that more than ever, lifting a bit up the rhythm: and it’s not “<em>a shame”</em> like Tonra sings, because it’s a brief parenthesis that closes on <em>Isolation</em>, where we feel the coming to an end of the EP, “<em>full-knowing we’re incomplete</em>”; and indeed the last song is <em>Neptune</em>, where we come back almost full circle to the Tonra of <strong>If You Leave </strong>(2013), but Tonra here seems to be a bit more liberated to experiment with her voice.  </p><p>If you need to put some faith in a record, you should start with this one: in the end, <em>there’s no-one</em> out there to <em>whisper answers</em>.
